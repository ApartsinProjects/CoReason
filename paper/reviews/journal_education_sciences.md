# Review: *Framing, Judging, Steering: An Assessable Competency Model for Teaching Students to Reason With Generative AI*

**Venue framing:** Reviewed as for a top learning-sciences / educational-psychology journal (e.g., *Educational Psychology Review*, *Journal of the Learning Sciences*, *British Journal of Educational Technology*), as a **conceptual / position contribution** with a proof-of-concept instrument.

**Reviewer stance:** learning sciences (metacognition, SRL, epistemic cognition, assessment / psychometrics, instructional design).

---

## 1. Summary

The paper proposes **CoReasoning**, a competency model that decomposes "productive work with generative AI" into three temporally and cognitively distinct, independently assessable skills: **Framing** (pre-generation task specification), **Judging** (post-generation evaluation of output), and **Steering** (post-generation iterative correction). The central structural claim is that prior frameworks fuse pre- and post-generation work into a single "prompting" competency, whereas CoReasoning separates them, with Judging as an epistemic gate between Framing and Steering. The decomposition is mapped onto a monitor–control metacognitive architecture (Nelson & Narens) seeded by an SRL task-definition phase (Winne & Hadwin), with Judging anchored in epistemic vigilance / apt epistemic performance (Sperber et al.; Barzilai & Chinn) and appropriate-reliance theory, and the overall pedagogical stance grounded in productive struggle / desirable difficulties (Kapur; Bjork & Bjork) and ZPD (Vygotsky). Five propositions (P1–P5) are stated. The model is instantiated in an open-source platform (CoReasoning Lab) and a 16-prompt LLM instrument. A feasibility demonstration with **simulated** learners (8 crossed competence profiles × 5 challenges = 40 learners; learners generated by gpt-4o-mini, graded by gpt-4o) reports that the three skills "dissociate" (own-skill grade effects exceed cross-skill effects ~60:1; near-zero inter-skill correlations) and that the grader is 92% self-consistent. The authors make **no learning-outcome claims** and present a prepared-but-not-run human-rater validation agenda.

The paper is unusually well-written, theory-literate, and scrupulously scoped. It is also, in its current form, carrying a structural mismatch between the strength of its rhetorical claims ("the skills dissociate") and what the evidence (synthetic, partly hard-coded, single grader family, N=40 with much of the variance attributable to design) can bear. For a learning-sciences venue specifically, the deeper issue is that **no learner, no learning, and no human judgment appear anywhere in the empirical material** — which is defensible for a conceptual paper but must be reflected far more honestly in the framing of the "central result."

---

## 2. Recommendation

**Major Revision** (for an education-sciences venue, as a conceptual / position paper).

The conceptual contribution is real and publishable; the theoretical scholarship is strong. But three things block acceptance as written: (a) the "dissociation" evidence is over-claimed relative to a design where one of the three skills (Judging) is hard-coded to dissociate and the other two rest on N=40 synthetic transcripts from one grader family; (b) several theory mappings are asserted as "load-bearing" but are actually decorative or, in two cases, slightly misread; (c) the operationalization of competence as **letter grades A/B/C** is psychometrically thin and is doing more work in the headline numbers than the paper acknowledges. None of these is fatal; all are fixable with reframing and added caveats rather than new data. Hence Major, not Reject.

I would not accept at Minor because the central empirical sentence of the abstract ("the three skills *dissociate* … by roughly sixty to one") needs to be materially weakened, and that is not a cosmetic edit.

---

## 3. Strengths

1. **A genuine and well-identified gap.** The diagnostic argument in Section 2 — that a single "prompting" score cannot tell an instructor *which* operation failed (mis-specification vs. failure to detect vs. failure to correct) — is the strongest part of the paper. It is the kind of move learning scientists value: it reframes an assessment problem in terms of separable, remediable failure modes. This is a real contribution to AI-literacy assessment thinking.

2. **The temporal-separation claim is non-trivial and correctly positioned.** Separating pre-generation Framing from post-generation Steering, with Judging as the gate, is a defensible conceptual wedge against 4D AI Fluency (Dakan & Feller) and prompt-literacy models (Lo; Tour & Zadorozhnyy). Table 1 is a model of how to do novelty positioning honestly. Section 6 is exemplary in confronting near-neighbors directly rather than strawmanning them.

3. **Scoping honesty about learning outcomes.** The repeated, explicit "we make no learning-outcome claims" is correct and commendable. The paper does not pretend the instrument teaches anything. For a field plagued by EdTech efficacy overclaiming, this restraint is a strength.

4. **The propositions raise the paper above taxonomy.** P1–P5, each with a named falsifier, is what turns a label set into a theory with empirical commitments. P3 (dissociation) and P4 (asymmetric transfer of Judging vs. Framing) in particular are interesting, testable, and non-obvious.

5. **Open artifact + prepared human-rater package.** Releasing the instrument, harness, data, and a pre-built agreement study (codebook, blinded rater files, scoring scripts) is good open-science practice and lowers the barrier for the field to do the validation the paper itself cannot.

## 4. Major concerns

### M1. The "dissociation" headline is over-claimed; one of the three skills is *constructed* to dissociate, and the paper's own caveats undercut its abstract.

The abstract and Section 8 lead with dissociation "by roughly sixty to one." But the paper later concedes (Section 8, "Scope and caveats," item i) that **Judging's competence is operationalized by a programmatic selection over ground-truth seeded issues** — "a strong judge flags all real issues and no false ones; a weak judge flags few real issues and some false ones." Judging's clean diagonal (+2.00, zero cross-effects) is therefore **a property of the experimental harness, not a discovered separation of constructs**. Yet Judging's +2.00 dominates the diagonal mean (+1.03) that produces the 60:1 ratio. Strip Judging out and the *blind-graded* evidence is Framing +0.60 and Steering +0.50 with off-diagonals near zero — real, but a far cry from 60:1, and resting on N=40 synthetic transcripts.

This is the single most important problem for a learning-sciences reviewer, because "the skills dissociate" is the load-bearing empirical claim against the "this is just relabeling prompting" charge. The 60:1 figure is, in effect, partly a tautology.

> Quote (§8): "The diagonal … averages **+1.03**; the off-diagonal … averages **−0.017**, so own-skill effects exceed cross-skill effects by … about 60 to 1."
> Quote (§8 caveats): "Judging's dissociation is partly built in … its clean diagonal (+2.00 …) confirms the grader correctly rewards recall/precision but is not, by itself, a discovered separation."

These two statements cannot both headline the paper. The second is correct; the first must be subordinated to it.

**Concrete fix.** (a) Recompute and lead with the dissociation ratio **excluding the hard-coded Judging row/column**, i.e., report the Framing/Steering blind-graded separation as the primary result and present Judging's diagonal explicitly as a *manipulation check on the grader*, not as evidence of construct separation. (b) Remove "sixty to one" from the abstract; replace with the honest blind-graded effect sizes (own-effects +0.50 to +0.60 on a 3-point scale, near-zero cross-effects, N=40 simulated) plus the explicit statement that Judging is a built-in control. (c) State plainly in the abstract that the dissociation evidence is from **simulated learners**, not humans — currently a reader must reach the methods to learn this.

---

### M2. Operationalizing competence as A/B/C letter grades is psychometrically inadequate for a dissociation argument, and the paper leans on it without justification.

Dissociation is a claim about **measurement**: that three indicators load on three factors, not one. The instrument's output is a **3-point ordinal letter grade per skill** (A=3, B=2, C=1). A three-level ordinal with a documented grader-leniency floor (§8 caveat iv: "weak steering is seldom graded all the way to C") is a coarse, ceiling/floor-prone scale on which to rest a separability claim. Several issues compound:

- With only **three grade indicators**, the paper concedes a factor model is "under-identified" (§8 caveat iii) and instead reports a single "first principal factor = 42% variance." But 42% from three coarse ordinal indicators over N=40 is weak evidence either way; with three indicators you cannot distinguish a one-factor from a three-factor model reliably. The "far below the near-100% a single 'AI-use ability' account would predict" framing sets up a strawman — no realistic single-factor model predicts ~100% shared variance across noisy 3-level grades.
- Treating A/B/C as interval (computing means and "+0.60" deltas, bootstrap CIs on grade differences) is the standard ordinal-as-interval sin. On a 3-point scale this is materially risky: a "+0.60" mean shift can be produced by a handful of B→A flips. The bootstrap CIs ([+0.38,+0.81] etc.) quantify resampling noise but not the ordinal-scale distortion.
- The grader-leniency floor on Steering means the **scale itself is non-uniform across skills**, so cross-skill grade comparisons (the off-diagonal) are confounded by per-skill scale compression.

**Concrete fix.** (a) Justify the 3-point scale or move to the underlying per-criterion ratings (the evaluator already produces "per-criterion ratings on a three-point scale" before aggregation — expose those as a richer continuous-ish score and run the dissociation on them). (b) Drop or heavily hedge the 42%-variance/PCA sentence; with three indicators it is not informative. (c) Report the dissociation with ordinal-appropriate statistics (e.g., effect on grade distribution, polychoric inter-skill correlations rather than Spearman on 3 levels) and acknowledge the ordinal-as-interval limitation explicitly.

---

### M3. No human learners and no human judgment: the conceptual claim outruns the evidence, and the gap should reshape the contribution, not just be noted.

The paper is honest that learners are simulated and grades unvalidated against humans. But for a learning-sciences venue the implication runs deeper than a limitation bullet. **A "competency model" is a claim about human learners' cognition.** The empirical material contains zero observations of human cognition: the "learners" are competence-conditioned prompt outputs, and the "dissociation" is a property of how a grader model responds to manipulated prompt inputs. What is demonstrated is that *an LLM grader's outputs separate when its inputs are manipulated per skill* — an instrument-behavior result, not a learner-cognition result. The crossed-profile "personas" (weak-framer/strong-judge etc.) are constructed by the authors; that a model can grade an artificially crossed profile as crossed is close to circular as evidence that the skills are psychologically distinct *in people*.

The risk is that a reader takes "the constructs dissociate" as a claim about learners. The paper sometimes invites this (e.g., abstract: "the three skills *dissociate*"; §6: "our feasibility demonstration supports … a learner's Framing and Steering grades can diverge" — but no learner produced those grades).

**Concrete fix.** (a) Systematically rephrase every dissociation claim as a claim about the **instrument's discriminant behavior under controlled inputs**, never about learners, throughout the abstract, contributions list, and §8. (b) In the contributions and conclusion, reframe the empirical contribution as "the instrument can score the three skills apart on controlled inputs (a necessary precondition for measuring them in learners)," not as evidence the constructs are separable in people. (c) Cite the one piece of genuine human evidence you already have (Clerc et al., 2026: behavioral regulation predicts use, self-rated expertise does not) more prominently as the *only* learner-level support, and be explicit that everything else is in-silico.

---

### M4. Two theory mappings are misread or overstated; one anchor is decorative.

The paper claims its anchors are "load-bearing," but several are looser than asserted:

1. **Nelson & Narens monitor/control (load-bearing, but the object level is mis-located).** The paper sets "the AI's generative process" as the object level the learner supervises (§3.1). But in Nelson & Narens the meta level holds a model *of the object level*, and monitoring/control regulate *the cognizer's own cognition*. Re-casting an **external** generative system as the object level is a substantive theoretical move (it makes metacognition exo-directed), not a straightforward instantiation. This may be defensible (it resembles "metacognition about a tool"), but it is currently asserted as if it were a literal fit. The paper should acknowledge that it is *extending* monitor–control to an external object, and engage the question of whether monitoring an external agent is the same construct as monitoring one's own memory/comprehension. As stated it slightly misreads N&N by treating the AI as if it were the learner's object-level cognition.

2. **Winne & Hadwin COPES task-definition (accurate and the best-supported anchor).** This mapping is the strongest; Framing-as-task-definition is faithful. No change needed beyond noting that COPES task definition is internal/recursive across phases, whereas Framing is treated as a one-shot upstream act — a simplification worth flagging.

3. **Productive struggle / desirable difficulties / ZPD (decorative and partly contradicted).** The pedagogical stance (§4, §9) leans on Kapur (productive failure), Bjork & Bjork (desirable difficulties), and Vygotsky (ZPD) to justify the "deliberately imperfect AI + judge/steer loop." This is the weakest theoretical link and matters because it is the *pedagogical* justification a learning-sciences reviewer scrutinizes most. Problems: (i) **Kapur's productive failure** specifically concerns learners *generating and exploring solutions before instruction* and failing productively at the *generation* step — it is not obviously the same as *critiquing a flawed externally supplied artifact*, which is closer to error-detection / worked-example-with-errors paradigms (e.g., erroneous examples; Große & Renkl). The paper cites Kapur as if "imperfect output to repair" were productive failure; it is arguably a different, well-studied paradigm the paper does not cite. (ii) **Desirable difficulties** is about retrieval/spacing/interleaving conditions that impair performance but aid retention; "friction of specifying, evaluating, correcting" is a loose analogy, not an instance. (iii) The **ZPD** framing requires a *more-capable other* scaffolding the learner toward independence; here the AI is *deliberately less* capable (seeded with flaws), and the learner supplies the correction. That is explicitly an **inversion** of the ZPD/cognitive-apprenticeship direction (the paper itself says "inversion of cognitive-apprenticeship coaching," §4) — which sits awkwardly with simultaneously invoking ZPD as if the AI extends the learner's reach. The paper needs to resolve whether the AI is a more-capable scaffold (ZPD) or a fallible artifact the learner repairs (inverted apprenticeship / erroneous examples); it cannot be both without argument.

**Concrete fix.** (a) For N&N, add one paragraph acknowledging the exo-directed extension and cite work on metacognition directed at external/social sources or distributed metacognition. (b) Replace or supplement Kapur with the **erroneous-examples / error-detection** literature (Große & Renkl; Siegler; Booth et al.), which is the actually-matching paradigm for "critique a flawed artifact," and reserve Kapur for any *generation-before-instruction* element if present in Framing. (c) Disambiguate the ZPD vs. inverted-apprenticeship tension explicitly: state which role the AI plays at which phase, and drop ZPD where the AI is the fallible repairee.

---

### M5. Construct boundary between Judging and Steering is argued but not empirically separable in the demo — and the P2/P3 reconciliation is logically delicate.

§3.3 carefully reconciles P2 (Judging upper-bounds Steering → positive correlation) with P3 (dissociation), via "ceiling relation induces correlation without identity." This is a clever argument but it has an unaddressed measurement consequence: **if Steering is bounded by Judging, then the Steering grade is partly a function of Judging quality**, which means the Steering indicator is *not* a clean measurement of a separable Steering construct — it is contaminated by Judging by design. The paper half-acknowledges this (§8 caveat iv: weak Steering own-effect is "partly the confound that Proposition P2 predicts"). But you cannot simultaneously claim (a) Steering dissociates from Judging (P3, low J–S correlation, ρ=+0.17) and (b) Judging bounds Steering (P2, which forces J–S correlation upward) without the data adjudicating which dominates — and at N=40 with a hard-coded Judging arm, it cannot. The +0.17 J–S correlation is also the *only* non-trivial inter-skill correlation, which is at least consistent with P2 contamination rather than clean separation.

**Concrete fix.** Acknowledge that in the present design Judging and Steering are *partially confounded by construction* (P2), so their low observed correlation is weak evidence of separability — possibly the weakest of the three pairs. Frame the clean Framing–Judging ρ=0.00 (pre- vs. post-generation, no gating relation) as the *strongest* dissociation pair and lead with it; treat J–S separability as an open question for the validation study with a stricter Steering rubric.

---

### M6. Missing engagement with assessment / psychometric validity theory and with errors-as-instruction literature.

For a learning-sciences journal, a paper that proposes an "assessable competency model" and reports a "dissociation" must engage modern validity theory (Messick; Kane's argument-based validity; AERA/APA/NCME *Standards*). The paper uses "construct validity" and "feasibility" informally but never frames its claims within a validity argument (what inferences from scores are warranted, what evidence supports each). The §10 agenda is about *agreement* (κ, α) — reliability/inter-rater — but inter-rater agreement is not construct validity, and the paper occasionally conflates the two. Relatedly, the **errors-as-instructional-material** tradition (erroneous examples, self-explanation of errors) is absent, despite being the closest pedagogical kin to the "deliberately flawed output" design (see M4).

**Concrete fix.** (a) Add a short paragraph framing the score-inference claims with Messick/Kane (content, substantive, structural, generalizability, external, consequential aspects), and map the present feasibility evidence to *one* facet (structural/internal) while the §10 studies target others. (b) Distinguish reliability (the 92% self-consistency, κ agreement) from validity explicitly; right now "92% self-consistent" risks reading as a validity claim. (c) Add the erroneous-examples literature to §4 and §7.

## 5. Minor issues

- **Abstract over-precision.** "roughly sixty to one" and "92% self-consistent" in the abstract convey a precision the synthetic N=40 single-grader design does not warrant. Soften and add "simulated learners."
- **§8 "near-100% a single 'AI-use ability' account would predict"** is a strawman; no one claims ~100% shared variance. Remove or restate as a directional expectation.
- **N=40 is small and confounded with challenges.** 8 profiles × 5 challenges means each cell is N=5 (or N=1 per profile-challenge). The bootstrap resamples cannot manufacture power the design lacks. State effective N per effect clearly.
- **"255 blind grading calls"** is described in token-budget terms; clarify these are not 255 independent learners (a reader may misread the effective sample size).
- **Self-consistency denominators.** "92% self-consistent" rests on 4 transcripts × 5 repeats. State N=4 transcripts prominently; it is currently buried and is a very small reliability base.
- **Judging ρ values reported to two decimals** (F–J ρ=0.00) on N=40 with 3-level grades imply false precision; report with CIs or as "indistinguishable from zero."
- **P5 (inverse of offloading)** is the most speculative proposition and is stated almost as established ("the framework offloads execution while retaining cognition"). It rests entirely on design intent, not evidence. Hedge in §4 and §9, not only in §5.
- **Bloom's "Create-level"/Flavell "metacognitive task knowledge"** (§4) are name-drops that add little; either operationalize their use or cut for concision.
- **ICAP "Interactive" claim** (§4): the paper notes itself (§9) that rapid AI dialogue can be "voluminous and shallow," undercutting the ICAP-Interactive claim. Integrate this caveat at first mention.
- **Hattie & Timperley "feed-forward"** mapping to Steering is apt but the learner is giving feedback *to the AI*, inverting the usual feedback recipient (learner). Note this inversion (it parallels the apprenticeship inversion).
- **Hebrew/English bilingual platform** is mentioned but all challenges are English (§11); the bilingual claim is unsupported by any evidence and could be cut or flagged as untested.
- **"92%" vs. flip-rate 0.08**: ensure the reader sees these are the same number; currently stated separately.
- **Reference hygiene:** several 2025–2026 arXiv preprints (Clerc et al. 2026; Di Santi 2026; Feng et al. 2025; Srinath et al. 2025) carry load in the argument; for an education journal, flag clearly which are peer-reviewed vs. preprint, since some claims (e.g., Clerc's human-learner result, used as the only human evidence) rest on non-peer-reviewed work.

---

## 6. Prioritized revision checklist

1. **(M1, blocking) Re-headline the dissociation result.** Remove "sixty to one" from abstract. Lead with blind-graded Framing/Steering own-effects (+0.60/+0.50) and present Judging's +2.00 explicitly as a hard-coded manipulation check, not a discovered dissociation. Recompute the ratio excluding Judging.
2. **(M3, blocking) Re-scope every dissociation claim as instrument behavior, not learner cognition.** Abstract, contributions, §6, §8, conclusion. State "simulated learners" in the abstract.
3. **(M2) Strengthen the measurement basis.** Run the dissociation on the underlying per-criterion ratings rather than 3-level letter grades; drop/hedge the 42%-variance PCA sentence; use ordinal-appropriate statistics and acknowledge ordinal-as-interval risk.
4. **(M4) Fix the theory mappings.** (a) Acknowledge the exo-directed extension of Nelson & Narens; (b) replace/supplement Kapur with the erroneous-examples / error-detection literature; (c) resolve the ZPD-vs-inverted-apprenticeship tension explicitly.
5. **(M5) Concede J–S partial confound.** Reframe Framing–Judging (ρ=0.00) as the strongest dissociation pair; treat Judging–Steering separability as open due to the P2 ceiling relation.
6. **(M6) Add a validity-theory frame.** Situate claims in Messick/Kane; separate reliability from validity explicitly; add erroneous-examples literature to §4/§7.
7. **(Minor) Report effective N honestly** (8×5, cells of 5; 4 transcripts for reliability), soften abstract precision, flag preprint vs. peer-reviewed sources, and prune decorative theory name-drops.

---

### Closing assessment

This is a thoughtful, well-scholared conceptual paper with a real and useful idea: the pre-/post-generation separation of Framing from Steering is a legitimate advance over existing AI-literacy and prompt-literacy frameworks, and the diagnostic-failure-modes argument is genuinely valuable to education research. The conceptual contribution would survive even if the empirical demo were removed entirely. The problem is that the empirical demo, as currently framed, claims more than it can deliver: a partly hard-coded dissociation, on coarse ordinal grades, from synthetic non-learners, scored by one grader family, headlined at "60:1." Reframed as a modest instrument-feasibility check subordinate to the conceptual contribution — and with the theory mappings tightened where they are decorative or misread — this becomes a strong conceptual paper appropriate for an education-sciences venue. The fixes are reframing and added rigor, not new experiments, so the revision is tractable.
