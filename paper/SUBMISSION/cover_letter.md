# Cover Letter

**To:** The Editors, *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence*
**Re:** Submission of a Position Paper

Dear Editors,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"Framing, Judging, Steering: An Assessable Competency Model
for Teaching Students to Reason With Generative AI,"** for consideration as a **Position Paper** in
*Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence*.

**The problem.** As generative AI is absorbed into education, most tools optimize for speed-to-answer,
and a growing body of evidence links uncritical AI use to cognitive offloading and weakened critical
thinking. Current AI-literacy instruments can often tell us *that* a learner's AI use is unproductive,
but not *why*, because they collapse distinct cognitive operations into a single "prompting" score.

**The contribution.** We propose **CoReasoning**, a competency model that decomposes productive work
with generative AI into three temporally and cognitively distinct, independently-assessable skills:
**Framing** (specifying an ill-defined task before generation), **Judging** (critically evaluating the
output), and **Steering** (iteratively correcting it). The model's defining novelty is the *separation
of the pre-generation skill (Framing) from the post-generation corrective skill (Steering)* that prior
frameworks (4D AI Fluency; the metacognitive-demands account; prompt-literacy models) fuse under
"prompting." We ground each skill in established theory (metacognitive monitoring and control,
self-regulated learning, epistemic vigilance, productive struggle), state five testable propositions,
and report a proof-of-concept instrument whose feasibility demonstration gives convergent and
discriminant validity evidence (a multitrait-multimethod analysis across three grader backends from two
vendors, and across shared- versus independent-competence populations) that the three skills are
separately measurable rather than one relabeled "prompting" skill.

**Fit with the venue.** The paper matches the journal's Position Paper type (a new conceptual
framework, not necessarily requiring primary empirical data) and speaks to a central current concern:
how to assess, not just deploy, generative-AI competence. It also contributes a reusable open-source
artifact: the scoring instrument, the data, and a prepared human-rater validation protocol.

**Scope.** The feasibility demonstration uses simulated learners (generated by one model and graded by
a different one to avoid self-grading) and establishes construct separability and automatic scoreability
of the instrument. Agreement with human expert graders and learning outcomes are the defined next stages
of the program, framed as an argument-based validity case (Messick; Kane); the human-rater agreement
study is fully prepared. The present evidence supports the scoring and generalization inferences, and we
specify the extrapolation and implication studies that follow.

**Originality and ethics.** This manuscript is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and has
no concurrent submission. It involves no human subjects and no personal data. All code, prompts, and
data are released under the MIT license.

Thank you for considering our submission.

Sincerely,
