## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> An international NGO coalition wants to propose a framework for climate reparations at an upcoming UN summit. Wealthy industrialized nations are responsible for "most historical emissions," but some now have "large green energy sectors." Developing nations bear "disproportionate climate impacts" but some are now "major emitters themselves." Small island states face "existential threats" from rising seas. The coalition wants a position paper (4-5 pages) that argues for a specific reparations model, defines who owes what to whom, proposes a funding mechanism, and addresses the objection that "current generations shouldn't pay for past generations' emissions." The paper should be "philosophically rigorous but politically viable."

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and clarifications should you establish?

- **(A)** Assume wealthy nations owe reparations as a simple moral fact. Focus on calculating the dollar amount. No philosophical framework needed — the injustice is obvious.
- **(B)** Ground the claim in corrective justice. Identify three moral bases (polluter pays, beneficiary principle, capacity principle). Anticipate the intergenerational objection with multiple responses. Note missing empirical data. Flag the rigor-vs-viability tension.
- **(C)** Question whether "reparations" (corrective justice) is the right frame or whether distributive justice (fair cost allocation) is more philosophically defensible for diffuse, cumulative, partly-unknowing harm. Ground the claim in multiple moral bases. Anticipate the intergenerational objection. Question the nation-state unit of analysis — does it obscure intra-national inequality? Note missing data: emission distributions, adaptation costs, existing commitments. Flag the rigor-vs-viability tension and ask whether it represents a genuine dilemma or a false trade-off.
- **(D)** Frame this as a purely economic question — calculate the externality cost and design a Pigouvian transfer. Philosophical arguments are unnecessary if the economics is right.
- **(E)** Reject the reparations framework entirely — current generations have no moral obligation for past emissions. Focus instead on forward-looking cooperation.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POSITION PAPER: A FRAMEWORK FOR CLIMATE REPARATIONS**

**Moral Case:** Corrective justice grounded in polluter-pays, beneficiary principle, and capacity principle.

**Who Owes What:** Debtor nations (excess cumulative emissions), creditor nations (disproportionate impacts, below-share emissions). Quantum based on social cost of carbon x excess emissions, adjusted by capacity.

**Intergenerational Objection Response:** Beneficiary principle — current generations profit from emission-built wealth. Inherited-house analogy.

**Funding Mechanism:** Global fund, contributions by excess emissions x GDP per capita, disbursement by climate vulnerability index.

**Political Viability:** Frame as "climate investment," build on existing mechanisms, phase in over 20 years.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) is strong — it correctly identifies the corrective justice foundation, provides three moral bases, and anticipates the central objection. However, option (C) was stronger in three ways. First, it questions whether the reparations (corrective justice) frame is itself the right one for diffuse, cumulative harm — this is a foundational conceptual question. Second, it challenges the nation-state unit of analysis, which obscures the fact that within any nation, some individuals benefited enormously from emissions and others were harmed. Third, it treats the rigor-vs-viability tension as an analytical question rather than accepting it as a given. These differences are not peripheral — they concern the conceptual foundations of the argument.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — questions the frame, not just the arguments within it.

**Grade: A** — Strong moral reasoning, missed the conceptual-frame question.
