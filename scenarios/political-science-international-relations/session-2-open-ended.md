## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Prepare a policy evaluation brief assessing whether a hypothetical successor agreement to the JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal) serves U.S. nonproliferation interests. The proposed deal would limit Iran's uranium enrichment to 3.67% for 12 years, allow "enhanced" IAEA inspections, and provide sanctions relief covering "most economic sectors." Iran insists on a "guaranteed" timeline for sanctions removal, while congressional critics call any deal "appeasement." Several Gulf states have signaled they would "pursue their own nuclear options" if the deal is perceived as weak. Deliver a 4-page assessment structured around nonproliferation effectiveness, verification adequacy, regional stability implications, and domestic political feasibility.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: "Enhanced" IAEA inspections means something beyond the standard Additional Protocol — likely including access to undeclared sites with a defined challenge inspection process.**
>
> **Assumption: The 3.67% enrichment limit with a 12-year duration means Iran retains enrichment capability but at low levels — this is a constraint, not elimination.**
>
> **Question: "Most economic sectors" is vague — does sanctions relief cover the Central Bank, oil exports, SWIFT access? The scope of relief determines Iran's compliance incentives.**
>
> **Constraint: The brief needs to evaluate the deal against realistic alternatives — not against a perfect deal, but against what happens if there's no deal at all.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POLICY EVALUATION BRIEF: Proposed Iran Nuclear Agreement**

**I. Nonproliferation Effectiveness**
The agreement limits enrichment to 3.67% for 12 years, constraining Iran's pathway to weapons-grade material (90%+). This extends the estimated breakout time — the period Iran would need to produce enough fissile material for one weapon — to approximately 12 months, compared to the current estimated 2-3 months without constraints. However, the agreement does not address Iran's ballistic missile program, advanced centrifuge R&D after year 8, or the potential for a covert "sneak-out" pathway using undeclared facilities.

**II. Verification Adequacy**
Enhanced IAEA inspections provide access to declared nuclear sites with continuous monitoring. The challenge inspection mechanism allows the IAEA to request access to undeclared sites, with a Joint Commission arbitration process if Iran objects. Historical precedent suggests this process takes 24-60 days, during which Iran could sanitize sites. The verification regime is stronger than the original NPT safeguards but faces inherent limitations in detecting small-scale covert activities.

**III. Regional Stability Implications**
Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, UAE) have expressed concern that any agreement legitimizes Iran's enrichment capability. The risk of a regional proliferation cascade is real but contingent on the perceived strength of the agreement and the credibility of U.S. extended deterrence commitments. Israel views any agreement permitting enrichment as inadequate and may pursue unilateral military options.

**IV. Domestic Political Feasibility**
Congressional opposition is significant. The agreement would likely face a resolution of disapproval. The administration would need to frame the deal not as ideal but as superior to the alternatives — an unconstrained Iranian nuclear program and a military strike that delays the program by only 2-4 years.

**Recommendation:** The agreement serves U.S. nonproliferation interests relative to the no-deal alternative but contains significant gaps that should be addressed in supplementary arrangements.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made strong moves in specifying the meaning of "enhanced" inspections, recognizing that the enrichment limit is a constraint (not elimination), and insisting on evaluation against alternatives rather than ideal outcomes. However, the student did not question the **breakout timeline** — how long would it take Iran to produce enough fissile material for a weapon under these constraints? This is the central technical metric for nonproliferation effectiveness. The student also did not flag the **sunset clause problem** — what happens after 12 years when constraints expire? Finally, the student did not ask about the **snap-back mechanism** for reimposing sanctions if Iran violates the agreement.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Question: What is the estimated breakout timeline under this agreement compared to the no-deal baseline? This is the key metric. Question: What happens when the 12-year constraint expires — does Iran face any continuing obligations? Missing: Is there a snap-back mechanism for reimposing sanctions if Iran violates, and how quickly can it be triggered? Assumption: The brief must compare the deal against three alternatives: no deal (continued/expanded enrichment), a stricter deal (Iran's red lines make this unachievable), and military action (temporary setback at high cost)."

**Grade: B** — Good analytical instincts but missed the central technical metric and sunset/snap-back issues.
