## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Build an impact assessment for a proposed comprehensive trade agreement between the European Union and Mercosur (Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay). The deal would eliminate tariffs on "over 90% of goods" over a 15-year phase-in period, open services markets to "greater competition," and include a sustainability chapter with "binding commitments" on deforestation. European farmers warn of being "undercut by cheap imports," while Mercosur industrial sectors fear "European manufactured goods flooding the market." Environmental groups say the sustainability provisions are "toothless." Produce a structured assessment covering trade creation and diversion effects, distributional impacts across sectors, environmental and labor implications, and a set of recommendations for negotiators on both sides.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: "Over 90% of goods" means the remaining 10% are sensitive sectors excluded or given extended transition periods — likely beef, ethanol, sugar on the EU side, and automotive, machinery on the Mercosur side.**
>
> **Question: What are the current tariff rates on the major traded goods between the two blocs? Without baseline tariff data, we can't estimate the magnitude of trade creation.**
>
> **Assumption: The 15-year phase-in period suggests the agreement anticipates adjustment costs — I'll treat this as evidence that short-run distributional impacts are expected to be significant.**
>
> **Question: What does "binding commitments" on deforestation mean operationally? Is there a monitoring mechanism, penalties for non-compliance, or a dispute settlement process?**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**EU-MERCOSUR TRADE AGREEMENT: IMPACT ASSESSMENT**

**I. Trade Creation and Diversion Effects**

*Trade Creation:*
The agreement would create new trade flows by reducing tariff barriers. Key sectors likely to see increased bilateral trade:
- EU exports to Mercosur: Machinery, automobiles, pharmaceuticals, chemicals (current Mercosur tariffs: 14-35%)
- Mercosur exports to EU: Beef, poultry, soybeans, ethanol, sugar, orange juice (current EU tariffs: 12-40% + quotas)

*Trade Diversion:*
The preferential tariff reductions would divert trade from third parties:
- Mercosur beef could displace Australian and New Zealand beef in the EU market
- EU automobiles could displace Japanese and South Korean vehicles in Mercosur
- Third-party effects should be assessed against WTO Most Favored Nation principles

**II. Distributional Impacts**

*EU Winners:* Industrial exporters (automotive, machinery, chemicals), services firms
*EU Losers:* Farmers (beef, poultry, sugar), especially in France, Ireland, Poland
*Mercosur Winners:* Agricultural exporters (ranchers, soy producers), commodity traders
*Mercosur Losers:* Manufacturing sectors (auto parts, machinery), domestic services firms

**III. Environmental and Labor Implications**

The sustainability chapter includes "binding commitments" on deforestation, but the enforceability is unclear. Brazil's deforestation rates have fluctuated significantly with domestic policy changes. The agreement could increase agricultural production incentives in Mercosur, potentially driving land-use change. Labor provisions typically mirror ILO core conventions but lack enforcement teeth in most trade agreements.

**IV. Recommendations**

*For EU negotiators:*
- Insist on enforceable sustainability chapter with trade sanctions for non-compliance
- Establish transition funds for affected agricultural regions
- Negotiate longer phase-in periods for the most sensitive products

*For Mercosur negotiators:*
- Secure meaningful market access for beef and ethanol (quotas, not just tariff reductions)
- Negotiate infant industry protections with defined sunset clauses for automotive sector
- Push for EU recognition of Mercosur food safety and quality standards

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student asked the right structural questions — identifying the sensitive sectors, requesting baseline tariff data, and questioning the enforceability of sustainability provisions. The inference about the 15-year phase-in signaling significant adjustment costs was a strong analytical move. However, the student did not specify an **analytical framework** — will the assessment use static welfare analysis, a computable general equilibrium model, or a qualitative political economy approach? The choice of framework determines what the assessment can credibly claim. The student also did not flag the **non-tariff barriers** (sanitary/phytosanitary standards, technical regulations, rules of origin) that often matter more than tariffs in EU trade agreements. Finally, the student did not question whether the agreement creates **regulatory convergence** pressures that go beyond simple tariff reduction.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Sensitive sectors (EU agriculture, Mercosur manufacturing) are likely excluded or phased in over 15 years. Question: What analytical framework should we use — static welfare, CGE model, or political economy? Each produces different answers. Missing: Non-tariff barriers (SPS standards, rules of origin) likely matter more than tariffs. Question: Does the sustainability chapter include a dispute settlement mechanism, trade sanctions for non-compliance, or only consultative processes? Missing: What are the rules of origin — how strict, and do they favor EU or Mercosur production chains?"

**Grade: B** — Good sectoral identification and enforcement questions, but missed the analytical framework and non-tariff barriers.
