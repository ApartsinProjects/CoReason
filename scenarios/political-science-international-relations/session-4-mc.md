## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Design a decision framework for a UN Security Council deliberation on whether to authorize military intervention in a fictional Central African country experiencing large-scale ethnic violence. Intelligence reports suggest "thousands have been killed" and "hundreds of thousands displaced," but the government claims it is conducting "legitimate counterterrorism operations." Two neighboring states have closed their borders, creating a refugee crisis. Russia and China have previously vetoed similar resolutions, citing "sovereignty concerns." Produce a structured recommendation document that includes criteria for triggering intervention, proposed force composition and mandate, a civilian protection plan, an exit strategy, and a diplomatic track to engage the host government and regional actors.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** Assume the R2P threshold is clearly met and intervention is morally obligatory. Focus the document on military planning. Ignore the veto problem — it is a political failure, not an analytical one.
- **(B)** Question the evidentiary basis for atrocity claims (source and verification). Recognize the dual possibility that counterterrorism and ethnic cleansing coexist. Note the Russia/China veto threat requires either a compromise strategy or alternative authorization pathway.
- **(C)** Question the evidentiary basis and verification methodology. Note that the R2P threshold itself must be established by the framework, not assumed. Map the host government's military capability and likely resistance. Identify the armed group landscape (factions, aims, potential spoilers). Assess terrain, logistics, and basing options. Plan for alternative authorization pathways if vetoed. Require force sizing to be justified by operational analysis, not arbitrary.
- **(D)** Assume intervention will fail based on historical precedents (Somalia, Libya). Focus the document on arguing against intervention. Recommend sanctions only.
- **(E)** The problem provides enough context — just ask the AI to produce a standard R2P recommendation document.

**Student selects: (C)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**RECOMMENDATION DOCUMENT: PROPOSED HUMANITARIAN INTERVENTION**

**I. R2P Threshold Determination Framework**

*Evidentiary Process:*
- Establish UN Commission of Inquiry (90-day mandate) with on-the-ground access
- Corroboration from at least two independent sources (ICRC, MSF, HRW, satellite imagery)
- Evidentiary standard: Finding of systematic and widespread attacks constituting crimes against humanity under Rome Statute Article 7
- Dual assessment: Evaluate both the government's counterterrorism claim and evidence of disproportionate attacks on civilians

*Threshold Criteria:*
- Scale: Evidence of killings exceeding 1,000 in defined time period
- Systematic nature: Pattern of targeting based on ethnicity, not military necessity
- State responsibility: Government forces directly involved or deliberately failing to protect

**II. Operational Feasibility Assessment**

*Host Government Capability:*
- Estimated 30,000-50,000 government troops, poorly equipped but with territorial knowledge
- Air assets: Limited helicopter fleet, no significant fixed-wing combat capability
- Likely resistance: Medium — government may acquiesce to intervention if sovereignty language is preserved

*Armed Group Landscape:*
- Primary ethnic militia: Estimated 5,000-10,000 fighters, loosely organized
- Government-aligned paramilitaries: 3,000-5,000, operating in rural areas
- Potential spoilers: Splinter factions and criminal networks exploiting instability

*Terrain and Logistics:*
- Semi-arid terrain with limited road network; seasonal flooding restricts movement
- Nearest viable staging base: Neighboring country (600 km from conflict zone)
- Airlift dependency: High; surface logistics unreliable

**III. Force Composition (Justified)**
- Population-to-force ratio: 1:150-200 for robust mandate (DPKO standard)
- Affected population: 2-3 million
- Required force: 15,000-20,000 troops + 2,000 police
- Air component: 16 fighter aircraft + 4 surveillance UAVs for no-fly zone
- Quick reaction force: 2 infantry battalions (1,500 troops) with helicopter support
- Primary contributors: AU member states (reduces sovereignty objections)
- External support: Logistics, intelligence, air assets from Western contributors

**IV. Mandate and Rules of Engagement**
- Mandate: Chapter VII authorization for protection of civilians, humanitarian corridor establishment, and monitoring of ceasefire
- ROE: Authorized to use force to protect civilians under imminent threat; authorized to respond to hostile actions against safe zones
- Scope limitation: Explicit exclusion of regime change; mandate focused on civilian protection and humanitarian access

**V. Civilian Protection Plan**
- Three safe zones around major population centers
- Humanitarian corridors linking safe zones to border crossings and airfields
- Civilian affairs coordination with OCHA, UNHCR, WFP

**VI. Exit Strategy**

| Benchmark | Indicator | Threshold | Monitor |
|---|---|---|---|
| Violence reduction | Civilian deaths/month | <50 for 3 consecutive months | OHCHR |
| Displacement reversal | IDP voluntary returns | 30% of displaced | UNHCR |
| Political process | National dialogue | All major groups at table | Special Envoy |
| Government reform | Independent judiciary | 3+ key appointments | AU-UN panel |

**VII. Diplomatic Track**
- AU mediation with host government
- Incentives: Sanctions relief, development assistance for compliance
- Engage neighboring states to reopen borders for humanitarian access

By requiring the R2P threshold to be established rather than assumed, demanding operational feasibility analysis, and insisting on justified force sizing, the student set conditions for a rigorous document. The output includes elements rarely seen in first-pass AI responses — host government capability assessment, armed group mapping, terrain analysis, and precedent-based force sizing.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** was the best choice.

**Grade: A** — Comprehensive framing that produced a substantially stronger initial output.

**VII. Diplomatic Track — Veto Mitigation (Revised)**

*Option A: Negotiated Compromise (Priority)*
- Proposed concessions to Russia/China: Time-limited mandate (6 months), explicit regime-change exclusion, host government consent process, economic cooperation provisions
- 30-day negotiation window
- Risk: Mandate may be weakened to ineffectiveness

*Option B: AU Regional Authorization (Fallback)*
- AU Constitutive Act Article 4(h) permits intervention for grave circumstances
- AU assembly authorization, then seek UN endorsement retroactively
- Precedent: AMISOM (Somalia)
- Risk: AU capacity limitations, uncertain legal standing

*Option C: UNGA Uniting for Peace (Legitimacy supplement)*
- Provides political legitimacy but not Chapter VII enforcement authority
- Used as supplement to Option B, not standalone
- Risk: No enforcement mechanism

*Recommended sequence: Pursue Option A with 30-day window. If vetoed, activate Option B with Option C for broader legitimacy.*

**Urgency vs. Due Process:**
- The 90-day Commission timeline creates a tension with ongoing atrocities
- Proposed solution: Deploy advance humanitarian mission (unarmed observers + humanitarian aid) under Chapter VI authority within 14 days while Commission conducts investigation
- This does not require the same level of UNSC consensus as Chapter VII enforcement
- Enables immediate presence and evidence collection while the formal process proceeds

**IV. Mandate — Separated Components**

*Component 1: Civilian Protection (Core)*
- Authorization: Chapter VII
- Force: 12,000 infantry, 2,000 police
- ROE: Authorized to use force to protect civilians under imminent threat
- Trigger: Immediate upon UNSC authorization

*Component 2: Humanitarian Corridors*
- Authorization: Chapter VII or Chapter VI with host government consent
- Force: 3,000 troops (engineers, logistics, security)
- ROE: Defend corridors but avoid offensive operations
- Trigger: Coordinated with OCHA humanitarian access plan

*Component 3: No-fly Zone (Escalation option)*
- Authorization: Separate UNSC resolution (higher political bar)
- Force: 16 aircraft, air defense systems, surveillance UAVs
- ROE: Enforce exclusion zone; engage only aircraft conducting attacks on civilians
- Trigger: Activated only if government conducts aerial attacks on civilians — not automatic

*Rationale for separation: The no-fly zone is the most politically contentious element. Separating it allows the civilian protection and corridor components to proceed even if the no-fly zone resolution is vetoed.*
