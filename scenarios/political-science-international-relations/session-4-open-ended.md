## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Design a decision framework for a UN Security Council deliberation on whether to authorize military intervention in a fictional Central African country experiencing large-scale ethnic violence. Intelligence reports suggest "thousands have been killed" and "hundreds of thousands displaced," but the government claims it is conducting "legitimate counterterrorism operations." Two neighboring states have closed their borders, creating a refugee crisis. Russia and China have previously vetoed similar resolutions, citing "sovereignty concerns." Produce a structured recommendation document that includes criteria for triggering intervention, proposed force composition and mandate, a civilian protection plan, an exit strategy, and a diplomatic track to engage the host government and regional actors.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The situation meets the R2P threshold — mass atrocities are occurring at a scale that constitutes a "manifest failure" of the host state to protect its population.**
>
> **Question: "Thousands killed" and "hundreds of thousands displaced" are vague — what is the evidentiary basis? Independent UN investigation, intelligence assessments, NGO reports, or media coverage? The source affects credibility.**
>
> **Constraint: Russia and China's veto threat means the framework must either address their concerns sufficiently to avoid a veto, or plan for alternative authorization (UNGA Uniting for Peace resolution, regional organization mandate).**
>
> **Question: The government's counterterrorism claim must be assessed — is there an actual armed insurgency, or is this pretext for ethnic cleansing? Both can be true simultaneously.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**RECOMMENDATION DOCUMENT: PROPOSED HUMANITARIAN INTERVENTION**

**I. Intervention Trigger Criteria**

The R2P framework requires that four conditions be met before military intervention is justified:
1. **Just Cause:** Mass atrocities occurring — confirmed by intelligence reports of thousands killed and hundreds of thousands displaced
2. **Right Authority:** UN Security Council authorization under Chapter VII
3. **Right Intention:** Primary purpose is civilian protection, not regime change
4. **Last Resort:** Diplomatic and economic measures have been exhausted or are manifestly inadequate

*Assessment: Conditions 1 and 3 appear met. Condition 4 depends on whether diplomatic efforts have been genuinely attempted. Condition 2 is the primary obstacle due to the veto threat.*

**II. Proposed Force Composition and Mandate**

- Force size: 15,000-20,000 troops (peacekeeping battalion plus quick reaction force)
- Mandate: Protection of civilians under imminent threat of physical violence; establishment of humanitarian corridors; monitoring of ceasefire if achieved
- Contributors: Primarily African Union member states (to reduce sovereignty objections), with logistical support from Western nations
- Duration: Initial 12-month mandate, renewable

**III. Civilian Protection Plan**

- Establish safe zones in three major population centers
- Deploy civilian affairs officers to coordinate with humanitarian agencies
- Create a no-fly zone to prevent aerial attacks on civilians

**IV. Exit Strategy**

- Transition to a UN peacekeeping mission within 18 months
- Benchmark: Reduction of violence to below mass atrocity threshold
- Political transition process initiated through inclusive national dialogue

**V. Diplomatic Track**

- Engage the host government through AU mediation
- Offer incentives for compliance: sanctions relief, development assistance
- Engage neighboring states to reopen borders for humanitarian access

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made two particularly strong moves: questioning the evidentiary basis for the atrocity claims (source credibility matters for UNSC deliberation) and recognizing the dual possibility that counterterrorism and ethnic cleansing can coexist. The veto-alternative framing was also strategically important. However, the student assumed too quickly that the R2P threshold is met — in practice, this is precisely what must be debated and established through evidence, not assumed. The student also did not question the **host government's military capabilities** and likely resistance to intervention, the **terrain and logistics** of the Central African context, or the **armed group landscape** (how many factions, what are their aims, are any potential partners?). These operational realities determine whether intervention is feasible, not just justified.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Question: What is the evidentiary basis for the atrocity claims — which sources, and what verification has occurred? The R2P threshold determination should be part of the framework, not assumed. Question: What is the host government's military capability and likely resistance? Missing: What is the armed group landscape — number of factions, aims, potential spoilers? Missing: What are the terrain, logistics, and basing options for an intervention force in this region? Constraint: Russia/China veto threat requires either a strategy to address their concerns or alternative authorization pathways."

**Grade: B** — Strong on evidentiary and authorization questions, but prematurely assumed the R2P threshold and missed operational feasibility.

#### Updated AI Output

**V. Diplomatic Track — Veto Mitigation Strategy (Revised)**

*Option A: Negotiate Compromise Resolution*
- Offer Russia/China: Time-limited mandate (6 months, renewable), explicit exclusion of regime change language, host government consent process, and economic cooperation provisions
- Risk: Compromise may water down the mandate to the point of ineffectiveness
- Precedent: Libya 2011 (UNSCR 1973) succeeded but eroded trust for future resolutions

*Option B: UNGA Uniting for Peace*
- If UNSC vetoed, invoke UNGA Uniting for Peace resolution (precedent: Suez 1956, Afghanistan 1980)
- Legal standing: Authorizes recommendations, not binding. Provides political legitimacy but not Chapter VII enforcement authority
- Risk: No enforcement mechanism; contributing states bear greater legal exposure

*Option C: Regional Organization Mandate*
- African Union Constitutive Act Article 4(h) permits AU intervention in member states for grave circumstances
- AU assembly authorization, then request UN endorsement retroactively
- Precedent: AU Mission in Somalia (AMISOM) preceded UN authorization
- Risk: AU capacity limitations; requires external logistical support

*Recommended Approach: Pursue Option A first with a 30-day negotiation window. If vetoed, pivot to Option C (AU authorization) with Option B (UNGA endorsement) for broader legitimacy. Prepare coalition-of-the-willing force commitments in parallel.*

**III. Civilian Protection — Safe Zone Rules of Engagement (Revised)**

- Safe zones established around three population centers with defined perimeters
- Rules of engagement: Intervention force authorized to use deadly force against any armed actor attacking or entering the safe zone without authorization
- Host government forces: If host government attacks a safe zone, the intervention force treats this as hostile action and responds accordingly, while diplomatic track immediately escalates
- No-fly zone enforcement: Requires dedicated air component — 12-16 fighter aircraft (contributed by capable AU partners or external supporters) plus surveillance UAVs
- Force composition updated: Add air component (16 aircraft + 4 UAVs) and air defense systems to the recommended 15,000-20,000 troop contingent
