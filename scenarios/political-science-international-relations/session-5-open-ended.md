## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a comprehensive post-conflict stabilization and state-building strategy for a fictional Southeast Asian country emerging from a five-year civil war between the central government and an ethnic separatist movement. A ceasefire has been signed but "trust is low on both sides." The country's economy has "collapsed," with GDP down "roughly 60%." International donors are willing to pledge "substantial aid" but want a "credible reform plan." The separatist region has been "self-governing" during the war and has its own armed forces, tax system, and courts. Neighboring states have "mixed interests" — some supported the government, others the separatists. Produce a phased strategy document covering security sector reform, transitional justice, power-sharing governance arrangements, economic reconstruction priorities, and a donor coordination framework.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The ceasefire is fragile — "trust is low" means both sides retain their military capabilities and are hedging against the possibility of resumed conflict.**
>
> **Constraint: The separatist region's self-governing institutions (armed forces, courts, tax system) cannot simply be dismantled — they are the basis of order in that region and dismantling them without replacement would create a power vacuum.**
>
> **Question: What is the demographic balance? If the separatist group is a small minority, a federal solution differs from one where they represent a significant portion of the population.**
>
> **Question: GDP down 60% means different things depending on the baseline — was this a low-income country before the war, or a middle-income one? The answer affects what economic reconstruction looks like.**
>
> **Assumption: "Mixed interests" among neighbors means some may actively support spoilers — the strategy must account for external interference.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POST-CONFLICT STABILIZATION STRATEGY**

**I. Security Sector Reform (SSR)**

*Phase 1 (Months 1-12): Ceasefire Consolidation*
- Cantonment of separatist armed forces in designated assembly areas
- Government forces withdraw from forward positions in the separatist region
- Joint Ceasefire Monitoring Commission (JCMC) established with international observers
- Small arms collection program in civilian areas (voluntary, incentive-based)

*Phase 2 (Months 12-36): Force Integration*
- Integrated national army: 60% government troops, 40% former separatist fighters (proportional to population)
- Joint training programs at unified military academy
- Retirement packages for excess personnel (estimated 15,000-20,000 fighters who cannot be absorbed)

**II. Transitional Justice**

- Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) established with international co-chairs
- Community-level reconciliation dialogues
- Amnesty for low-level combatants; ICC referral for senior commanders responsible for documented atrocities
- Reparations program for conflict-affected communities funded by international donors

**III. Power-Sharing Governance**

- Transitional government of national unity for 24 months
- Asymmetric federalism: separatist region retains education, health, and cultural affairs autonomy; defense, foreign affairs, and monetary policy centralized
- National legislature: Upper house with equal regional representation; lower house proportional to population
- Ethnic balance requirements for civil service, judiciary, and security forces

**IV. Economic Reconstruction**

- Emergency: Food security, basic infrastructure repair (roads, electricity, water)
- Short-term: Reactivation of agricultural production, cash-for-work programs
- Medium-term: Industrial recovery, foreign investment framework, trade normalization with neighbors
- Revenue sharing formula: Natural resource revenues split between central and regional governments

**V. Donor Coordination Framework**

- Lead coordination body: UN Resident Coordinator
- Sector working groups for each reform track
- Trust fund mechanism for pooled donor contributions
- Results framework with quarterly reporting

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong analytical moves. Recognizing the fragility of the ceasefire and the impossibility of simply dismantling separatist institutions showed sophisticated understanding of post-conflict dynamics. The demographic question was important — power-sharing formulas depend heavily on the population ratio. The economic baseline question was also analytically sharp. However, the student did not address the **credible commitment problem** — the central challenge in post-conflict settings. Why would the separatists disarm if they cannot trust the government to honor its commitments? What mechanism enforces the agreement if one side defects? The student also did not map the **spoiler landscape** — which factions within each side oppose the peace process, and what are their incentives? Finally, the student did not question the **sequencing** of reforms — should security come first, or governance, or economic recovery? The order matters because each track creates preconditions for others.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Constraint: Separatist institutions cannot be dismantled — they must be transformed. Question: What mechanism solves the credible commitment problem — how does each side trust the other to follow through on disarmament and power-sharing? Missing: Who are the spoilers within each faction — hardliners, war profiteers, external backers — and how can they be managed? Question: What is the right sequencing — does SSR precede governance reform, or must governance reform come first to create the institutional framework for SSR?"

**Grade: A** — Strong institutional and demographic analysis, but missed the credible commitment problem, spoiler mapping, and sequencing logic.

#### Updated AI Output

**I. Security Sector Reform — Credible Commitment Mechanisms (Revised)**

*Why Would Separatists Disarm?*

The credible commitment problem is the central challenge. Proposed mechanisms:

1. **Third-Party Security Guarantee:** An ASEAN-led observer force (3,000 troops) deployed along the line of separation during the transition period. The observer force has a Chapter VI mandate to report violations and serve as a tripwire — any attack on cantoned forces would be an attack on the international presence.

2. **Phased Reciprocal Disarmament:** Neither side fully disarms in Phase 1. Instead:
   - Month 1-6: Both sides withdraw heavy weapons to verified storage sites (monitored by JCMC)
   - Month 6-12: Both sides reduce active personnel by 30% (documented with biometric registration)
   - Month 12-24: Cantonment of remaining separatist forces begins only after government forces have completed equivalent drawdowns
   - Key principle: Symmetry of vulnerability — neither side is more exposed than the other at any phase

3. **Constitutional Lock-In:** The power-sharing and autonomy provisions are embedded in a new constitution ratified by both a national referendum and a separate regional referendum in the separatist area. Constitutional amendments require supermajority from both regions, making unilateral rollback legally and politically costly.

*Force Integration Ratio — Justification:*
- Population ratio: Separatist ethnic group is approximately 35% of national population
- Military balance at ceasefire: Roughly equal fighting capability
- Proposed ratio: 60/40 reflects a compromise — closer to population proportionality than military parity, but with guaranteed separatist representation at all command levels (division commander, brigade commander)
- Chain of command: Integrated units below brigade level; joint command structure at division level and above; Chief of Defense Staff rotates between groups for the first 10 years

**II. Transitional Justice — Amnesty/Accountability Calibration (Revised)**

*Threshold Mechanism:*
- **Tier 1 (Senior leaders — top 50-100):** Full accountability — ICC referral or special tribunal for crimes against humanity and war crimes
- **Tier 2 (Mid-level commanders — 500-1,000):** Conditional amnesty — amnesty granted in exchange for full testimony before the TRC, asset disclosure, and participation in community reconciliation. Amnesty revoked if testimony is found to be materially false.
- **Tier 3 (Rank-and-file combatants — 30,000+):** General amnesty upon completion of DDR (disarmament, demobilization, reintegration) program

*Spoiler Mitigation:*
- Tier 2 is the critical population. These individuals have enough power to destabilize the process but are not prominent enough for ICC attention.
- The conditional amnesty offers them a credible path to civilian life while extracting truth for the TRC.
- For those who refuse: Arrest warrants issued, assets frozen, travel bans imposed. International support for extradition if they flee to neighboring states.

#### Updated AI Output

**IV. Economic Reconstruction — Parallel System Integration (Revised)**

*The Problem:*
The separatist region has operated an independent economic system for 5 years: its own tax collection, trade networks (likely including cross-border trade with sympathetic neighbors), and possibly its own scrip currency. These systems currently sustain livelihoods for ~35% of the population. Dismantling them without a functional replacement would cause a second economic collapse.

*Proposed Approach: Gradual Convergence, Not Immediate Unification*

- **Phase 1 (Months 1-12): Parallel Operation**
  - Both tax systems continue operating
  - Joint Economic Commission established to map revenue flows, trade patterns, and institutional capacity in both systems
  - Monetary policy: If separatist scrip exists, establish a managed exchange rate with the national currency (pegged initially, allowed to converge over 12 months)
  - Cross-border trade: Formalize existing trade networks rather than criminalize them; register traders, apply harmonized tariffs

- **Phase 2 (Months 12-24): Harmonization**
  - Unified tax code negotiated with separatist region input
  - Revenue-sharing formula: 40% to central government, 40% to regions, 20% to equalization fund for underdeveloped areas
  - National currency adopted region-wide; separatist scrip phased out over 6 months with exchange guarantee
  - Joint customs and border management at international borders

- **Phase 3 (Months 24-48): Integration**
  - Single national fiscal framework fully operational
  - Investment promotion: Special economic zones in the separatist region to attract FDI and create employment for former combatants
  - Infrastructure: Reconnect road and rail networks severed during the war; prioritize routes linking the separatist region to national markets

*Natural Resource Revenue Sharing (Specified):*
- If the separatist region contains significant natural resources (timber, minerals, oil/gas): revenues split 50/30/20 (region/central/equalization fund) for the first 10 years, transitioning to the standard formula thereafter
- Transparent revenue management: Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI) compliance required

**V. Donor Coordination — Conditionality Framework (Revised)**

*Benchmark-Based Funding Tranches:*

| Tranche | Timing | Reform Benchmark | Funding Released |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Months 1-6 | JCMC operational, cantonment begins, TRC established | 20% of pledged funds |
| 2 | Months 6-12 | Reciprocal disarmament Phase 1 complete, transitional government seated | 25% |
| 3 | Months 12-24 | Force integration underway, economic harmonization begun, TRC hearings started | 30% |
| 4 | Months 24-36 | Constitutional process initiated, elections scheduled, unified fiscal framework | 25% |

*If Benchmarks Missed:*
- Missed by <3 months: Warning and technical assistance offered
- Missed by 3-6 months: Funding suspended (not cancelled); resumed upon compliance
- Missed by >6 months: Donor conference to reassess strategy; funds may be redirected to humanitarian assistance rather than reform programs
- Key principle: Conditionality should incentivize reform, not collapse the process. Suspension, not cancellation, preserves the incentive to catch up.
