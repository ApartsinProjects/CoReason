## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a comprehensive post-conflict stabilization and state-building strategy for a fictional Southeast Asian country emerging from a five-year civil war between the central government and an ethnic separatist movement. A ceasefire has been signed but "trust is low on both sides." The country's economy has "collapsed," with GDP down "roughly 60%." International donors are willing to pledge "substantial aid" but want a "credible reform plan." The separatist region has been "self-governing" during the war and has its own armed forces, tax system, and courts. Neighboring states have "mixed interests" — some supported the government, others the separatists. Produce a phased strategy document covering security sector reform, transitional justice, power-sharing governance arrangements, economic reconstruction priorities, and a donor coordination framework.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** Assume the ceasefire will hold. Focus on building democratic institutions as quickly as possible. The separatist institutions should be dismantled immediately since they are illegitimate.
- **(B)** Assume the ceasefire is fragile. Recognize that separatist institutions cannot simply be dismantled without creating a power vacuum. Question the demographic balance and economic baseline. Note that neighboring states with "mixed interests" may support spoilers.
- **(C)** Assume the ceasefire is fragile. Recognize separatist institutions must be transformed, not dismantled. Ask what mechanism solves the credible commitment problem — how does each side trust the other to follow through? Map the spoiler landscape — hardliners, war profiteers, external backers within each faction. Question the sequencing logic: does SSR come before governance reform, or must governance create the framework for SSR first? Assess which neighboring states are potential facilitators vs. potential spoilers.
- **(D)** Assume the war will resume regardless and plan for partition. The country should be split along ethnic lines.
- **(E)** The problem provides enough detail. Ask the AI to produce a standard post-conflict strategy document.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POST-CONFLICT STABILIZATION STRATEGY**

**I. Security Sector Reform**

*Phase 1 (Months 1-12):* Cantonment of separatist forces, government withdrawal from forward positions, Joint Ceasefire Monitoring Commission with international observers
*Phase 2 (Months 12-36):* Force integration — 60/40 government/separatist ratio, joint training, retirement packages for excess personnel

**II. Transitional Justice**
- Truth and Reconciliation Commission with international co-chairs
- Amnesty for low-level combatants; ICC referral for senior leaders
- Reparations program for conflict-affected communities

**III. Power-Sharing Governance**
- 24-month transitional government of national unity
- Asymmetric federalism: separatist region retains education, health, cultural autonomy
- National legislature with equal regional representation in upper house

**IV. Economic Reconstruction**
- Emergency: Food security, infrastructure repair
- Short-term: Agricultural reactivation, cash-for-work
- Medium-term: Industrial recovery, FDI framework
- Revenue sharing for natural resources

**V. Donor Coordination**
- UN Resident Coordinator as lead
- Sector working groups
- Trust fund for pooled contributions
- Quarterly results reporting

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) was reasonable but missed several critical dimensions that option (C) covered. Option (B) correctly identified the ceasefire fragility, separatist institution problem, and neighbor interference risk. However, option (C) additionally addressed the credible commitment problem (the central analytical challenge), mapped the spoiler landscape, questioned reform sequencing logic, and distinguished between facilitator and spoiler neighbors. These are not secondary concerns — they determine whether the strategy is implementable.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — the credible commitment problem, spoiler mapping, and sequencing logic are essential foundations.

**Grade: B** — Solid but missing the central analytical challenges.

#### Updated AI Output

**I. Security Sector Reform — Credible Commitment (Revised)**

*Credible Commitment Mechanisms:*
1. **ASEAN Observer Force:** 3,000 troops deployed along the line of separation; serves as tripwire against ceasefire violations
2. **Phased Reciprocal Disarmament:**
   - Months 1-6: Both sides withdraw heavy weapons to verified storage
   - Months 6-12: Both sides reduce active personnel by 30%
   - Months 12-24: Cantonment begins after equivalent government drawdowns
   - Principle: Symmetry of vulnerability
3. **Constitutional Lock-In:** Power-sharing embedded in new constitution ratified by national and regional referenda; amendments require supermajority from both regions

*Force Ratio Justification:*
- Separatist ethnic group: ~35% of population
- Military balance at ceasefire: Roughly equal
- 60/40 ratio: Compromise between population proportionality and military parity
- Guaranteed separatist representation at all command levels through division commander

**II. Transitional Justice — Tiered Accountability (Revised)**

- **Tier 1 (Senior, ~50-100):** Full accountability — ICC referral or special tribunal
- **Tier 2 (Mid-level, ~500-1,000):** Conditional amnesty in exchange for full TRC testimony and asset disclosure; amnesty revoked for materially false testimony
- **Tier 3 (Rank-and-file, ~30,000+):** General amnesty upon DDR completion

*Spoiler mitigation for Tier 2:* Conditional amnesty offers a credible path to civilian life. Those who refuse face arrest warrants, asset freezes, and travel bans.
