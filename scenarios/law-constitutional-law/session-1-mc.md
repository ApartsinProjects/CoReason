## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Draft a legal brief for a state attorney general defending a recently enacted statute that prohibits social media platforms from using algorithmic amplification of "harmful content" directed at users under 18. A coalition of tech companies has filed suit claiming the law violates the First Amendment. The statute defines harmful content as material that is "detrimental to the mental health of minors." Several states have passed "similar laws" with "mixed results" in court. Produce a 10-page brief with a statement of facts, legal argument, and proposed standard of review. The AG's office wants to "win this one" and is open to "creative arguments."

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates the brief?

- **(A)** Assume the statute is clearly constitutional under the government's police power. Focus the brief on policy arguments about why social media harms children. No need to address the standard of review — the court will figure it out.
- **(B)** Assume the statute regulates algorithmic amplification (conduct) rather than content (speech). Flag that "detrimental to mental health" is vague. Identify Moody v. NetChoice as key precedent. Assume some legislative findings exist.
- **(C)** Assume the statute targets amplification not content, question whether courts will accept that distinction, ask whether the legislative record has empirical evidence sufficient for strict scrutiny, flag the compelled speech angle, identify both Moody v. NetChoice and Reed v. Town of Gilbert as key precedent, question third-party standing and dormant Commerce Clause issues
- **(D)** Assume the First Amendment does not apply to corporations — they have no speech rights. Build the brief around the argument that platforms are common carriers with no editorial discretion.
- **(E)** Focus entirely on Section 230 immunity as the dispositive issue. The First Amendment analysis is secondary.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**BRIEF IN SUPPORT OF DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO DISMISS**

**I. Statement of Facts**
The State Legislature enacted HB 1247 following two years of hearings documenting the effects of algorithmic content amplification on minor users. The statute prohibits covered platforms from deploying algorithmic systems that amplify content classified as detrimental to the mental health of users under 18.

**II. Legal Argument**

*A. The Statute Regulates Conduct, Not Speech*
HB 1247 targets the automated amplification mechanism — a business practice — not any particular expression. Under this framing, rational basis review applies.

*B. Alternatively, Intermediate Scrutiny Is Satisfied*
Even if algorithmic curation constitutes protected editorial judgment, the statute is a content-neutral regulation of the manner of distribution under O'Brien.

*C. The State Has a Compelling Interest in Protecting Minors*
Under Ginsberg v. New York, the state has broader authority to regulate speech directed at minors.

**III. Proposed Standard of Review**
Rational basis, or alternatively intermediate scrutiny under O'Brien.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) is solid but incomplete. It correctly focuses on the speech/conduct distinction and flags the vagueness issue. However, option (C) was stronger because it anticipated that courts may reject the conduct characterization, asked whether the evidence supports strict scrutiny (a fallback the brief needs), and identified additional issues — compelled speech, third-party standing, and dormant Commerce Clause — that a thorough brief must at least acknowledge. In litigation, failing to anticipate the court's likely framework is a critical error.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — it covers everything in (B) plus the compelled speech angle, evidentiary sufficiency, and ancillary constitutional issues.

**Grade: A** — Good doctrinal instinct, but did not anticipate the full range of issues the court will consider.

#### Updated AI Output

**II. Legal Argument (Revised)**

*A. The Statute Is Content-Based but Survives Strict Scrutiny*
The State acknowledges that HB 1247 requires reference to content characteristics to identify "harmful" material, rendering it content-based under Reed v. Town of Gilbert. However, strict scrutiny is satisfied. The compelling interest in protecting minors' psychological well-being is supported by a two-year legislative record including longitudinal studies and internal platform documents. The statute is narrowly tailored because it restricts only the amplification mechanism, not the underlying content.

*B. Ginsberg's Framework Extends Beyond Obscenity*
Ginsberg v. New York's rationale rested on the state's "independent interest in the well-being of its youth" — not solely on the obscenity doctrine. Just as Ginsberg permitted restricting the manner of sale, HB 1247 restricts the manner of algorithmic distribution without banning content.
