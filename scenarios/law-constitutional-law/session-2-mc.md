## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Prepare an Office of Legal Counsel-style memorandum analyzing whether the President may, without congressional authorization, order the military to seize and operate privately owned semiconductor fabrication facilities during a declared national security emergency. A foreign adversary has imposed an export ban that cuts off "critical chip supplies," and the President's national security advisors say the shortage will "cripple defense systems within six months." The memo should address the constitutional basis for such action, applicable statutory frameworks, and relevant precedent. Congress has "not acted" on proposed emergency procurement legislation. Some members of Congress have publicly opposed executive seizure.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates the memorandum?

- **(A)** Assume the President has absolute authority as Commander-in-Chief during a declared emergency. No need to analyze congressional power — the President can act unilaterally in national security matters. Focus the memo on the breadth of Article II.
- **(B)** Identify Youngstown as the controlling framework. Determine whether congressional inaction places this in Category 2 or 3. Note the DPA as a potential statutory basis. Recognize the OLC memo genre requires balanced analysis. Do not address the Takings Clause.
- **(C)** Identify Youngstown as the controlling framework. Analyze whether congressional opposition moves this to Category 3. Examine DPA, National Emergencies Act, and IEEPA as potential statutory bases. Flag the Fifth Amendment Takings Clause as an independent constraint. Distinguish temporary seizure from permanent taking. Note the OLC genre requires presenting counterarguments.
- **(D)** Treat this as a simple property law question — analyze under eminent domain doctrine only. The separation of powers issue is secondary.
- **(E)** Assume Congress has implicitly authorized the seizure through its past defense appropriations. Apply Category 1 analysis.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**OFFICE OF LEGAL COUNSEL MEMORANDUM**

**RE:** Presidential Authority to Seize Semiconductor Fabrication Facilities

**III. Analysis**

*A. Constitutional Basis:* Article II Commander-in-Chief clause, executive vesting clause, and Take Care clause support broad emergency action.

*B. Youngstown Framework:* DPA authorizes priority ordering but not physical seizure. Action falls in Category 2 (congressional silence). President may rely on inherent Article II authority, but Youngstown's steel seizure precedent presents a substantial obstacle.

*C. Conclusion:* Seizure is "most defensible" as Category 2, supported by Commander-in-Chief authority.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) correctly identified Youngstown and the DPA, which are the two most important analytical frameworks. However, option (C) was stronger in three respects: it flagged the Takings Clause (physical seizure of private property triggers Fifth Amendment analysis regardless of the Article II question), it identified additional statutory authorities (National Emergencies Act, IEEPA) that a thorough OLC memo must consider, and it distinguished temporary from permanent seizure — a distinction that affects both the constitutional analysis and available remedies.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — covers Youngstown plus the Takings Clause, additional statutes, and temporal distinctions.

**Grade: A** — Strong primary framework identification, but missed the Takings Clause and alternative statutory authorities.

#### Updated AI Output

**III. Analysis (Revised)**

*B. Youngstown Framework (Revised):* The most defensible assessment places this in Category 3. Congress had proposed emergency procurement legislation and declined to act; individual members publicly opposed seizure. Under Dames & Moore, this constitutes implied disapproval. At the lowest ebb, presidential power is sustainable only if exclusively presidential. The Commander-in-Chief clause is the strongest basis, but Youngstown itself rejected domestic industrial seizure under that clause.

*C. Fifth Amendment Takings:* Under Loretto, physical military occupation of a fabrication facility is a per se taking requiring just compensation. Temporary seizure does not eliminate but reduces the compensation measure to fair rental value plus diminution. Critically, funding compensation may require congressional appropriation — reintroducing the congressional cooperation the President seeks to bypass.

*D. Conclusion:* The seizure faces Category 3 Youngstown scrutiny, adverse steel seizure precedent, and Takings Clause compensation obligations. The legally defensible alternative is DPA priority ordering under Title I.
