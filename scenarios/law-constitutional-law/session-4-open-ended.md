### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Write a policy analysis and legal memorandum for a state governor's legal counsel assessing whether the state's newly enacted cannabis licensing and regulatory framework can survive a federal preemption challenge. The state has legalized commercial cannabis cultivation, processing, and retail sale with a "comprehensive regulatory scheme." Federal law still classifies cannabis as a Schedule I controlled substance. The U.S. Attorney for the district has made "no public statements" about enforcement priorities. The governor wants to know "how vulnerable" the state program is and what "protective measures" the state can adopt.

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**MEMORANDUM FOR THE GOVERNOR'S LEGAL COUNSEL**

**RE:** Vulnerability of State Cannabis Regulatory Framework to Federal Preemption

**I. Executive Summary**
The state's cannabis legalization framework faces moderate legal risk from a federal preemption challenge. While conflict preemption is the most viable federal theory, several doctrines — including the anti-commandeering principle and the presumption against preemption of traditional state police powers — provide substantial defenses.

**II. Preemption Analysis**

*A. Express Preemption*
The CSA does not contain an express preemption clause directed at state cannabis laws. The CSA's savings clause, 21 U.S.C. § 903, provides that the CSA does not preempt state law unless there is a "positive conflict" between federal and state law "so that the two cannot consistently stand together." This savings clause limits preemption to conflict preemption.

*B. Field Preemption*
Field preemption is unlikely. The CSA's savings clause demonstrates congressional intent not to occupy the entire field of controlled substance regulation. Courts have consistently held that states retain authority to enact their own drug laws, including decriminalization measures.

*C. Conflict Preemption*
This is the strongest federal argument. A positive conflict exists when compliance with both federal and state law is impossible. Here, the state licensing framework authorizes and regulates conduct (cultivation, processing, sale) that federal law prohibits. A licensed cultivator cannot simultaneously comply with the state license (which requires cultivation) and the CSA (which prohibits it).

However, most courts have rejected preemption challenges to state cannabis laws. The leading analysis holds that there is no positive conflict because the state law does not require anyone to cultivate cannabis — it merely permits and regulates it. A person can comply with both laws by choosing not to cultivate. The state is not compelling violation of federal law.

*D. Protective Measures*
1. Ensure the state regulatory framework is permissive, not mandatory — no one is required to participate.
2. Include a disclaimer that state licensing does not immunize participants from federal prosecution.
3. Establish a state banking program for cannabis businesses.
4. Monitor federal enforcement posture and adjust program accordingly.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student's framing was well-structured. Correctly identifying the preemption taxonomy (express, field, conflict) and the savings clause was the right starting point. The anti-commandeering observation was relevant. Distinguishing the banking issue as a practical rather than doctrinal matter showed clear thinking. However, the student did not question whether the state's "comprehensive regulatory scheme" — which creates a licensing apparatus, imposes quality standards, and collects taxes — goes beyond mere decriminalization into active state facilitation of federally prohibited activity. This distinction matters because some courts have distinguished between states that merely remove criminal penalties and states that affirmatively license and regulate the prohibited conduct. The student also did not flag the Gonzales v. Raich Commerce Clause issue — Congress clearly has Commerce Clause authority over controlled substances, which strengthens the federal preemption argument. Finally, the student did not consider whether federal spending conditions (threatening to withhold federal grants from states with legalization programs) represent a separate vulnerability.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Supremacy Clause analysis with CSA conflict preemption as the primary theory. Question: Does the state's 'comprehensive regulatory scheme' go beyond decriminalization into affirmative facilitation? Courts may treat licensing and regulation differently from mere removal of state penalties. Question: How does Gonzales v. Raich's broad Commerce Clause holding affect the preemption analysis? Constraint: The anti-commandeering doctrine protects the state from being compelled to enforce the CSA but may not shield affirmative authorization. Gap: Federal spending conditions — could Congress threaten grant funding as leverage independent of preemption?"

#### Updated AI Output

**II. Preemption Analysis (Revised)**

*C. Conflict Preemption (Expanded)*
The conflict preemption analysis depends critically on how the state's legalization framework is characterized.

*Decriminalization alone:* If a state merely removes criminal penalties for cannabis possession and use, most courts have found no preemption. The state is not authorizing federally prohibited conduct — it is simply choosing not to punish it under state law. There is no positive conflict because individuals can comply with both laws by abstaining.

*Affirmative licensing and regulation:* The analysis becomes more complex when the state creates a comprehensive licensing framework that authorizes, regulates, and taxes commercial cannabis operations. In Emerald Steel Fabricators, Inc. v. Bureau of Labor & Industries (2010), the Oregon Supreme Court held that a state law requiring employers to accommodate medical marijuana use was preempted by the CSA because it affirmatively required conduct that federal law prohibited. The critical distinction was between permitting (not preempted) and requiring (preempted).

The state's framework occupies a middle ground: it does not require anyone to grow cannabis, but it creates a state apparatus that affirmatively licenses, inspects, and taxes commercial operations. This goes beyond decriminalization. The strongest argument against preemption is that state licensing does not compel anyone to engage in federally prohibited conduct — participation is voluntary. The strongest argument for preemption is that the state is actively facilitating and profiting from an industry that federal law prohibits, creating an irreconcilable conflict between the state's regulatory authority and the federal prohibition.

*D. Anti-Commandeering as Constitutional Defense*
Under Printz v. United States (1997) and Murphy v. NCAA (2018), the federal government cannot commandeer state legislatures by compelling them to enact or enforce federal regulatory programs. This principle provides a constitutional floor: whatever the preemption analysis concludes, the federal government cannot force the state to recriminalize cannabis.

However, the anti-commandeering doctrine protects state sovereignty over state law — it does not immunize individuals from direct federal enforcement of the CSA. State-licensed businesses and their customers remain subject to federal prosecution under the CSA regardless of state law.

The practical implications: (1) the state program can continue to operate as a matter of state law; (2) the federal government cannot enjoin the state from administering its licensing program; (3) but the federal government can prosecute individual participants, creating a deterrent effect that undermines the program's viability without requiring preemption.

#### Updated AI Output

**II. Preemption Analysis (Further Revised)**

*A. The CSA Savings Clause as the Textual Anchor*
21 U.S.C. § 903 provides that the CSA does not preempt state law except where there is a "positive conflict between [the CSA] and [state] law so that the two cannot consistently stand together." This language adopts the narrower "impossibility" standard for preemption rather than the broader "obstacle" standard sometimes applied in other statutory contexts.

Under the impossibility standard, the state must be requiring conduct that federal law prohibits, or prohibiting conduct that federal law requires. The state's cannabis framework does neither: it permits and regulates voluntary commercial activity but does not require anyone to cultivate, process, or sell cannabis. A person can comply with both the state law (by choosing not to participate) and the CSA (by not possessing cannabis). The "positive conflict" threshold of § 903 is not met.

The stronger federal argument is obstacle preemption — the state licensing framework frustrates the federal objective of eliminating cannabis from commerce. But § 903's text forecloses this argument. Congress chose to limit CSA preemption to positive conflicts, implicitly accepting that states might adopt laws inconsistent with federal drug policy short of actually requiring CSA violations.

*E. Federal Spending Power as Separate Vulnerability*
Independent of preemption, Congress could condition federal funding on states maintaining cannabis prohibitions. Under South Dakota v. Dole, spending conditions are valid if: (1) they serve the general welfare; (2) they are unambiguous; (3) they are related to the federal interest in the program; (4) they do not violate other constitutional provisions; and (5) they are not coercive.

The coercion limitation from NFIB v. Sebelius (2012) constrains this power. If Congress threatened to withhold a substantial portion of a state's federal funding (e.g., all Medicaid funding) unless the state recriminalized cannabis, this would likely constitute unconstitutional coercion — a "gun to the head" of the states. However, more modest conditions (e.g., withholding a portion of law enforcement grants) would likely survive. The governor should assess which federal funding streams are most at risk and their significance to the state budget.
