## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Design a 12-month leadership development program for approximately 60 middle managers at a fast-growing fintech company (tripled headcount in two years, now at 1,800 employees). Most managers were "promoted because they were great individual contributors" and have "had no formal management training." The CHRO says engagement survey scores for "manager effectiveness" are "in the bottom quartile" of the industry benchmark. Employee turnover among direct reports of these managers is "significantly higher than average." The company culture values "speed and disruption" and people are "skeptical of corporate training programs." Deliver a program architecture document, a competency framework, a learning journey map, and an ROI measurement plan.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The 60 managers span different functions (engineering, product, operations, sales) and have varying team sizes, so a one-size-fits-all program won't work.**
>
> **Assumption: "Skeptical of corporate training" means traditional classroom workshops will be poorly received. The program needs to feel practical, peer-driven, and directly relevant to daily work.**
>
> **Constraint: In a fast-growing company, managers can't be taken offline for extended periods. Learning must be integrated into the workflow, not stacked on top of it.**
>
> **Question: What does "manager effectiveness" mean in the engagement survey — is it about communication, feedback, career development, workload management, or all of the above? Without knowing which dimensions scored lowest, we're guessing at what to develop.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Competency Framework:**

| Competency | Description | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Coaching conversations | Give developmental feedback, conduct effective 1:1s | Directly drives engagement and retention |
| Team building | Hire well, onboard effectively, build psychological safety | Critical during rapid growth |
| Priority setting | Help team focus amid competing demands | Prevents burnout in fast-paced culture |
| Conflict resolution | Address interpersonal issues before they escalate | Reduces turnover from toxic dynamics |
| Strategic communication | Translate company strategy to team-level goals | Connects individual work to company mission |

**Program Architecture (12 months):**

| Month | Theme | Format | Time Commitment |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Self-assessment + cohort launch | 360 feedback, cohort kickoff (half-day) | 6 hours |
| 2-3 | Coaching conversations | 2-hour workshop + practice with real direct reports | 4 hours/month |
| 4-5 | Team building & psychological safety | Peer learning circles (groups of 5), case discussions | 4 hours/month |
| 6 | Mid-point reflection | Individual coaching session + peer feedback | 3 hours |
| 7-8 | Priority setting & delegation | Simulation exercise + manager-of-manager shadowing | 4 hours/month |
| 9-10 | Conflict resolution | Role-play workshops + real-case debriefs | 4 hours/month |
| 11-12 | Strategic communication | Capstone project: present team strategy to senior leadership | 6 hours/month |

**ROI Measurement Plan:**
- Leading: 360 feedback score improvement at 6 and 12 months
- Lagging: Team-level engagement scores, turnover rates for managed teams, promotion rates of direct reports
- Business: Revenue per employee in managed teams, time-to-productivity for new hires

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong moves — differentiating by function, identifying the cultural constraint against traditional training, noting the time-constraint reality, and asking which specific competency gaps the survey data reveals. These are practical, design-relevant questions. However, the student did not ask about the manager-of-manager layer — who manages these 60 managers, and are those senior leaders equipped to reinforce the development? Without manager-of-manager support, behavior change decays after training. The student also did not question whether all 60 managers should be in the same program — some may need basic management fundamentals while others need advanced coaching skills. A diagnostic should precede the curriculum design. Additionally, the student did not ask about the budget, which determines whether individual coaching (expensive) or peer-only formats (cheaper) are feasible.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Different functions and team sizes require differentiation. Question: Which specific engagement dimensions scored lowest — this should drive competency priorities. Question: Who manages the 60 managers, and will those senior leaders actively reinforce new behaviors? Without this, training won't transfer. Question: Should all 60 be in one program or segmented by baseline skill level? A pre-program diagnostic would tell us. Question: What is the budget — individual coaching for 60 people is expensive. Constraint: Time-sensitive culture means learning must be integrated into workflow."

**Grade: B** — Good practical instincts on cultural fit and time constraints, but missed the manager-of-manager layer, segmentation question, and budget.

### Summary Feedback

The student demonstrated good practical instincts throughout — correctly identifying the cultural constraint against traditional training, the need for data-grounded competency selection, and the importance of manager-of-manager accountability. Judging improved across cycles, with the segmentation and ROI attribution catches showing increasing analytical sophistication. Steering was consistently strong — corrections were specific, data-oriented, and produced implementable program design elements rather than abstract principles. The main weakness was in framing, where the student missed several feasibility and design questions (budget, segmentation, manager-of-manager layer) that had to be surfaced during later judging cycles. To improve, practice asking three questions during framing: "Who else needs to be involved for this to work?" (stakeholder mapping), "Are all participants the same?" (segmentation), and "What resources does this require?" (feasibility constraints).
