## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Design an intervention plan for a 12-person cross-functional product development team at a consumer electronics company that has "missed its last three deadlines" and whose members "don't trust each other." The team includes engineers, designers, and marketers spread across offices in San Jose and Shenzhen. The team lead (an engineer) was "promoted from within" six months ago and "some people don't respect her authority." Two senior engineers "dominate every meeting" while others "stay silent." HR has received "a few informal complaints" about the team atmosphere. Deliver a diagnostic framework, a sequence of structured interventions, and success metrics with a 90-day evaluation plan.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: "Don't trust each other" manifests as information withholding, blame when things go wrong, and reluctance to admit mistakes or ask for help.**
>
> **Assumption: The two dominant senior engineers are in San Jose (the headquarters) and the silent members are disproportionately in Shenzhen — the cross-site dynamic amplifies the power imbalance.**
>
> **Constraint: The team lead's authority deficit is partly structural (promoted from peer group, no formal leadership training) and partly relational (the dominant engineers may have been passed over or feel they should have the role).**
>
> **Question: Are the missed deadlines caused by the interpersonal dysfunction, or are there also structural issues (unclear requirements, scope creep, resource constraints) that would cause delays regardless of team dynamics?**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Diagnostic Framework:**

| Diagnostic Layer | Method | What It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| Individual | Confidential 1:1 interviews (all 12 members) | Personal concerns, perceived causes of dysfunction, trust levels, career intentions |
| Dyadic | Relationship mapping (who works with whom, who avoids whom) | Key relationship fractures, information bottlenecks |
| Subgroup | Faultline analysis (San Jose vs Shenzhen, function, tenure) | Whether dysfunction follows predictable group boundaries |
| Team-level | Team psychological safety survey (Edmondson's 7 items) | Baseline safety score, comparison to norms |
| Structural | Process audit of last 3 missed deadlines | Root causes: people problems vs process/scope/resource problems |

**Intervention Sequence:**

1. **Week 1-2: Diagnosis** — Interviews, survey, process audit
2. **Week 3: Team lead coaching** — 4-session coaching engagement focused on authority establishment, facilitation skills, managing dominant voices
3. **Week 4-5: Structured dialogue** — Facilitated team session to surface issues using a structured format (each person speaks uninterrupted, no rebuttals)
4. **Week 6-8: Operating norms redesign** — Team co-creates new meeting norms, decision-making protocols, and cross-site communication rhythms
5. **Week 9-12: Sustained practice** — Weekly team retrospectives with rotating facilitators, continued coaching for team lead

**Success Metrics:**
- Psychological safety survey score: target 15% improvement from baseline at 90 days
- Meeting participation balance: Gini coefficient of speaking time drops below 0.3
- Deadline adherence: next product milestone met on time
- HR complaints: zero new informal complaints in 90 days

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong moves — operationalizing "don't trust each other" into specific behaviors, hypothesizing the cross-site power dynamic, and critically asking whether structural causes also contribute to missed deadlines. The distinction between structural and relational sources of the team lead's authority deficit was also insightful. However, the student did not ask about the HR complaints — "a few informal complaints" could range from interpersonal friction to allegations of bullying, harassment, or discrimination, which would fundamentally change the intervention approach (from team development to investigation and potential personnel action). The student also did not question whether the team lead wants to remain in the role — if she is considering stepping down, the intervention design changes significantly.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Trust deficit manifests as information withholding, blame, reluctance to be vulnerable. Question: What are the HR complaints about specifically — interpersonal friction, or allegations of bullying/harassment? This determines whether we intervene with team development or begin with investigation. Question: Does the team lead want to remain in the role? Assumption: Cross-site power imbalance with headquarters dominance. Question: Are missed deadlines purely relational or also structural (scope, requirements, resources)? Constraint: Team lead needs both structural empowerment and skill development."

**Grade: A** — Strong behavioral analysis and structural thinking, but missed the critical question about the nature of HR complaints.
