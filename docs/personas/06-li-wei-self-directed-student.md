## Background

Li Wei is a motivated student who supplements assigned challenges with self-created private challenges for extra practice. He uses CoReasoning Lab not just for course requirements but as a personal skill-building tool. He creates his own challenges on topics where he feels weak, iterating until his grades improve.

## Behavioral Patterns

### Private Challenge Creation
Li Wei creates 1-2 private challenges per week, targeting specific subject tree nodes where he scored poorly on public challenges. He values the ability to:
- Choose his own subject focus
- Practice in low-stakes mode (no instructor visibility)
- Retry until he understands the pattern
- See how rubric criteria apply to different problems in the same subject area

### Learning Pattern
Li Wei's improvement trajectory is visible in his grade history:
- **Weeks 1-3:** Consistent C in judging — he focused too narrowly on surface-level errors
- **Weeks 4-6:** Judging improved to B after reading the "what else could be wrong?" feedback pattern repeatedly across multiple challenges
- **Current:** Stable B across all three skills, working toward A in framing

He explicitly uses the feedback to guide his next attempt, sometimes taking notes on the "better alternative" suggestions.

### Open-Ended Preference
Li Wei prefers open-ended mode for all three response points because it forces him to generate his own analysis rather than recognizing correct options. He sees MC mode as "easier but less useful for learning."

## Use Cases for System Evaluation

1. **Private challenge pedagogical value:** Do private challenges (with the same rubric generation and feedback quality as public challenges) support genuine self-directed learning?

2. **Improvement tracking across private challenges:** Can Li Wei see his own improvement trajectory? Does the student analytics view (My Results) help him identify which skills are improving and which are plateauing?

3. **Challenge creation for students:** Is the challenge creation flow intuitive enough for students? Do students understand the Phase/MC/Open-ended configuration options, or are they designed primarily for instructors?

4. **Subject tree navigation:** Does Li Wei find it easy to target specific subject tree nodes for his weaknesses? Does the tree structure align with how students think about topics they need to practice?

5. **Open-ended response structure:** Does the named-sections format (gaps, instructions) help Li Wei organize his thinking? Or is the structure constraining for a student who might want to write freeform analysis?

## Evaluation Scenarios

- **Scenario A:** Li Wei creates a private challenge on "Dynamic Programming > Optimization > Knapsack." He gets a C on judging. He reads the feedback, creates another Knapsack challenge, and tries again. Does the second problem test the same rubric criteria but with different content? Does his performance improve?
- **Scenario B:** Li Wei reviews his "My Results" page and filters by course. He notices his judging grade has improved from C to B over 6 challenges. Is this improvement visible and motivating? Does the interface help him set a goal (e.g., "reach A in judging for Algorithms")?
- **Scenario C:** Li Wei creates a challenge with open-ended judging. He adds 5 named gaps. Two are accurate, one is a non-issue, and two are partially correct. Does the grading system handle this mixed-quality response correctly? Does the feedback address each gap individually or give aggregate feedback?
