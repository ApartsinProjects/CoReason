What is Co-Reasoning?

Learn to think critically with AI — not just use it

The Problem

Most people use AI passively: paste a question, copy the answer. But AI outputs often contain subtle errors, missing assumptions, and logical gaps. If you can't spot these issues, you're not using AI — you're being used by it. Co-Reasoning teaches you to work with AI as a critical partner, not a crutch.

The Three Skills

Co-Reasoning develops three independent critical thinking skills:

🎯

Framing

Turn vague problems into well-defined tasks. Real problems are messy — you must identify what's missing, add assumptions, and clarify requirements before AI can help.

🔍

Judging

Find what's wrong with AI's answer. AI solutions look good but contain subtle errors. Your job is to identify incorrect assumptions, missing edge cases, and logical flaws.

🎮

Steering

Guide AI toward a better answer. Once you've found issues, give the AI specific, actionable corrections — not vague complaints.

How a Challenge Works

Each challenge walks you through the three skills in sequence:

Read an intentionally ill-defined problem statement
Frame it by selecting or writing refinements to clarify the problem Graded (A/B/C)
See an AI-generated solution that is deliberately imperfect
Judge the issues — identify flaws, incorrect assumptions, and missing cases Graded (A/B/C)
Steer with specific, actionable corrections to improve the solution Graded (A/B/C)
Review your three independent grades and detailed feedback

Why Three Grades?

Each skill is graded independently because they are distinct competencies. You might frame a problem brilliantly but miss obvious flaws in the AI's answer, or judge well but give vague steering feedback. Three separate grades give you a clear picture of where to improve.

A
Excellent
Thorough, precise, and insightful
B
Good
Solid understanding with minor gaps
C
Needs Work
Significant issues to address

Practice vs Assessment

Challenges come in two modes:

Practice Mode

Run as many times as you want. See detailed feedback and rubric explanations after each phase. Great for learning and experimentation — your best attempt counts.

Assessment Mode

One attempt only. Grades are final and reported to your instructor. Designed for evaluating your skills under realistic conditions. Treat it like an exam.
