## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a three-year DEI strategy for a global management consulting firm (approximately 12,000 employees across 30 offices in 15 countries). Women represent "about 45% of entry-level hires" but only "12% of partners." Racial and ethnic diversity data is "available in some countries but not others" due to legal restrictions. The firm's recent employee survey found that "many people from underrepresented groups feel they have to work twice as hard to be recognized." Several high-profile departures of diverse senior leaders made "industry news." The managing partner wants to "move beyond programs and fix the systems." Deliver a systemic barrier analysis framework, a set of policy and process interventions targeting the three highest-leverage barriers, and an accountability and measurement architecture.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** The problem is just about hiring — increase entry-level diversity targets to 60% women and the pipeline will fix itself over time. Ignore the partner gap for now.
- **(B)** The 45%-to-12% drop occurs at specific transition points in the pipeline. "Fix the systems" means reforming evaluation, promotion, staffing, and sponsorship processes, not adding programs. Racial/ethnic data varies by jurisdiction. Question what drove senior departures.
- **(C)** The pipeline drop occurs at specific transitions. "Fix the systems" means process reform. Data varies by jurisdiction. Question what drove departures. Additionally: account for intersectionality (gender gap is worse for women of color). Examine client-facing staffing as a driver of inequitable career development. Distinguish between bias-reduction (training) and system-design interventions (process architecture).
- **(D)** Launch an unconscious bias training program for all 12,000 employees — this will solve the partner gap within three years.
- **(E)** The senior departures are normal turnover — no systemic issues exist. The managing partner is overreacting to media coverage.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Systemic Barrier Analysis Framework:**

| Process | Decision Points | Barrier Mechanisms | Data Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Performance evaluation | Rating, calibration | Ambiguous criteria, biased recall, "executive presence" norms | Rating distributions by demographic |
| Promotion | Nomination, dossier review, committee vote | Informal gatekeeping, inconsistent standards, "culture fit" | Promotion rates by cohort/demographic |
| Work allocation | Project staffing | High-visibility projects go to "known quantities" | Staffing by project tier and demographic |
| Sponsorship | Informal advocacy in talent reviews | Affinity-based sponsorship | Sponsor-protege match data |
| Flexible work | Leave, return-to-work | Career penalty for leave-taking | Leave-taker promotion rates |

**Top 3 Interventions:**
1. Structured performance evaluation (behaviorally anchored scales, written evidence, blind calibration)
2. Transparent promotion process (published criteria, diverse slates, independent reviewer)
3. Equitable work allocation (quarterly staffing audit, stretch assignment tracker)

**Accountability:** Annual dashboard, practice leader targets, external advisory board.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) is solid — it correctly identifies the pipeline drop-off, interprets "fix the systems" as process reform, acknowledges data constraints, and asks about departure causes. However, option (C) was stronger on three important dimensions: it raises intersectionality (the gender gap is likely much steeper for women of color), highlights client-facing staffing as a specific barrier mechanism unique to consulting, and distinguishes between bias-reduction training and system-design interventions. These are not minor additions — intersectionality prevents treating "women" as a monolithic category, client staffing is arguably the highest-leverage point in a consulting firm, and the bias-training vs system-design distinction is central to the managing partner's stated goal of "fixing the systems."

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — covers everything in (B) plus the three dimensions that most determine whether a DEI strategy produces systemic change or symbolic activity.

**Grade: B** — Good structural framing, but missed the dimensions that distinguish systemic from programmatic approaches.

#### Updated AI Output

**Intervention 4: Client-Facing Staffing Equity**

| Component | Design |
|---|---|
| Quarterly audit | Project assignments reviewed by demographic and tier (Tier 1: marquee, Tier 2: mid-market, Tier 3: routine). Flag when any group underrepresented in Tier 1 by > 10pp vs cohort share. |
| Stretch tracker | For manager+ level: track Tier 1 engagements, client ownership, revenue attribution by cohort demographic. |
| Staffing prompt | Diversity indicator shown to staffing managers when building teams — awareness, not veto. |

**Intersectional Measurement:**

| Context | Approach |
|---|---|
| Full data (US, UK) | Disaggregate promotion, attrition, inclusion by gender x race, gender x parenting status |
| Limited data (EU) | Opt-in self-identification, qualitative experience audits with intersectional sampling |
| No data | Process compliance metrics, exit interview analysis, regional advisory panels |

**Partner Buy-In Framing:**
- Position reforms as improving meritocracy: "We're reducing noise in evaluation — ambiguous criteria and undocumented decisions introduce randomness that hurts everyone's ability to identify the best talent."
- Present data: "When evaluation criteria are vague, ratings correlate more with evaluator identity than with performance. Structured evaluation is more accurate, not less rigorous."
- Enlist respected senior partners as reform champions: "This isn't about lowering the bar — it's about making sure the bar is consistently applied."
- Address directly: "If our current system is truly meritocratic, structured evaluation and transparent promotion will confirm that. If it's not, we want to know."
