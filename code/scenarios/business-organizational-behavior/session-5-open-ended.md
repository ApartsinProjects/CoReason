## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a three-year DEI strategy for a global management consulting firm (approximately 12,000 employees across 30 offices in 15 countries). Women represent "about 45% of entry-level hires" but only "12% of partners." Racial and ethnic diversity data is "available in some countries but not others" due to legal restrictions. The firm's recent employee survey found that "many people from underrepresented groups feel they have to work twice as hard to be recognized." Several high-profile departures of diverse senior leaders made "industry news." The managing partner wants to "move beyond programs and fix the systems." Deliver a systemic barrier analysis framework, a set of policy and process interventions targeting the three highest-leverage barriers, and an accountability and measurement architecture.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The 45%-to-12% pipeline drop is not uniform — the biggest attrition points are likely at the transition from manager to senior manager and from senior manager to partner. Identifying where the biggest drop occurs tells us where to focus.**
>
> **Assumption: "Fix the systems" means reforming the actual decision-making processes — performance evaluation criteria, promotion committee composition and procedures, work allocation and staffing algorithms, and sponsorship — not adding mentoring programs or bias training on top of unchanged systems.**
>
> **Constraint: Racial/ethnic diversity data is unavailable in some jurisdictions (e.g., France, Germany), so the measurement architecture must work with different data granularity across regions. Gender data is likely available everywhere.**
>
> **Question: What drove the senior departures? If diverse leaders left because they were passed over for partner despite strong performance, that's a promotion system problem. If they left because of day-to-day exclusion or lack of belonging, that's a culture and inclusion problem. These require different interventions.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Systemic Barrier Analysis Framework:**

| Process Under Review | Key Decision Points | Potential Barrier Mechanisms | Data Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| Performance evaluation | Annual rating, calibration committee | Ambiguous criteria favor "executive presence" norms; raters' biased recall of contributions; calibration anchored on first rating mentioned | Rating distributions by demographic, calibration observation data |
| Promotion decisions | Nomination, dossier review, committee vote | Informal nomination gatekeeping; inconsistent evidence standards; "culture fit" as unstated criterion | Promotion rates by cohort and demographic, committee composition records |
| Work allocation / staffing | Project assignment, client staffing | High-visibility projects go to "known quantities"; diverse consultants staffed on smaller or less prestigious engagements | Staffing data by project tier and demographic, utilization rates |
| Sponsorship and advocacy | Informal relationships, who speaks up in talent reviews | Affinity-based sponsorship; leaders sponsor people who remind them of themselves | Sponsor-protege demographic match data, career velocity by sponsorship status |
| Flexible work and parenting | Leave policies, return-to-work, part-time options | Career penalty for leave-taking; assumption that part-time = not committed | Leave-taker promotion rates vs peers, post-return attrition |

**Top 3 Highest-Leverage Interventions:**

1. **Structured Performance Evaluation Reform:** Replace ambiguous criteria with behaviorally anchored rating scales tied to specific competencies. Require written evidence for each rating. Calibration committees review ratings blind (demographic information removed) before discussion.

2. **Transparent Promotion Process:** Publish promotion criteria. Require diverse slates for partner nominations. Add an independent reviewer to each promotion committee who assesses decision quality (not the candidates, but the committee's reasoning). Track and publish aggregate promotion rates by demographic.

3. **Equitable Work Allocation:** Audit project staffing data quarterly for demographic patterns. Implement a staffing algorithm that flags when consultants from underrepresented groups are consistently assigned to lower-tier projects. Create a "stretch assignment" tracker that ensures equitable access to career-accelerating engagements.

**Accountability and Measurement:**
- Annual diversity dashboard published internally
- Practice leaders accountable for improvement targets
- External advisory board reviews progress annually

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong analytical moves — identifying the pipeline drop-off points as the key diagnostic question, interpreting "fix the systems" as process reform rather than programmatic add-ons, recognizing the data availability constraint across jurisdictions, and asking about the root causes of senior departures. This framing set the AI up well to focus on structural interventions. However, the student did not address intersectionality — the 45%-to-12% gender gap is likely more severe for women of color, women from certain cultural backgrounds, or women with caregiving responsibilities. Treating gender as a single category misses compounding barriers. The student also did not ask about the firm's client-facing model — in consulting, client preferences and client-team matching are a significant (and uncomfortable) source of bias that is rarely addressed because it's attributed to "client relationships."

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Biggest drop-off at manager-to-senior-manager and senior-manager-to-partner transitions. Assumption: 'Fix the systems' means reforming evaluation, promotion, staffing, and sponsorship processes. Constraint: Data varies by jurisdiction. Question: What caused the senior departures — promotion bypass or daily exclusion? Question: Does the analysis account for intersectionality — is the gender gap more severe for women of color or women from specific backgrounds? Question: How does the firm's client-facing model contribute to inequitable staffing — are diverse consultants systematically assigned to different client types?"

**Grade: A** — Strong structural analysis, but missed intersectionality and the client-facing staffing dimension.

#### Updated AI Output

**Accountability Architecture:**

| Component | Specification |
|---|---|
| **Targets** | Promotion rate parity: ratio of promotion rates (underrepresented / majority) must reach 0.85-1.15 band within 3 years. Retention parity: attrition rate differential < 3 percentage points. Experience parity: inclusion index score gap < 10% between demographic groups. |
| **Timeline** | Year 1: Establish baselines, launch process reforms. Year 2: Measurable improvement on leading indicators (calibration audit findings, staffing equity scores). Year 3: Outcome targets met. |
| **Consequences** | Practice leaders' bonus pool weighted 15% on DEI metrics. Published annual scorecard per practice/office (internal). Leaders who meet targets recognized publicly; leaders who miss targets without credible improvement plan face compensation reduction. |
| **Data-restricted regions** | Where demographic data cannot be collected: use proxy measures (interview inclusion index, qualitative experience audits, anonymized focus groups). Regional leaders report on process compliance (e.g., % of calibration sessions with trained facilitator) rather than demographic outcomes. Annual assessment by external advisory board of whether regional approach is adequate. |
| **Governance** | DEI steering committee (managing partner + 3 practice leaders + CHRO + 2 external advisors) meets quarterly. Committee reviews dashboard, approves target adjustments, and holds escalation authority. |

**Revised Calibration Process (replacing blind calibration):**

| Stage | What Happens | Bias Mitigation Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-calibration | Raters submit written evidence for each rating using standardized template (specific examples, not trait labels) | Forces documentation, reduces recency/availability bias |
| Calibration discussion | Trained facilitator leads discussion. Each candidate discussed in randomized order against the same rubric. Equal time allocated per candidate. | Prevents "first discussed = most discussed" anchoring |
| Real-time challenge | Facilitator flags: (a) trait-based language without behavioral evidence ("she lacks executive presence"), (b) double standards (different evidence bar for different candidates), (c) "potential" claims without observable indicators | Makes bias visible in the moment |
| Post-calibration audit | HR analytics team checks: rating distributions by demographic, promotion recommendation rates, distribution of narrative themes by demographic (NLP-assisted text analysis) | Detects patterns invisible in individual discussions |
| Feedback loop | Audit results shared with calibration committee and practice leaders within 30 days. Patterns requiring action flagged to DEI steering committee. | Creates accountability and learning |

#### Judging Feedback

**Criticism:** Both catches were important and well-reasoned. The accountability section criticism was essential — vague accountability is the most common reason DEI strategies fail. The blind calibration impracticality was a sophisticated observation that shows understanding of how consulting firms actually operate. However, the student did not flag that the three interventions all focus on internal processes but none address client-facing staffing decisions, which in consulting firms are a major driver of inequitable career development. Who gets staffed on the most prestigious client engagements determines who builds the relationships and revenue track record needed for partner promotion. The student also did not note that the strategy lacks an explicit intersectionality lens — the 45%-to-12% gap is likely far steeper for women of color.

**Better Alternative Judgement:**
> "Gap: Accountability section is too vague. Error: Blind calibration is impractical in a firm where people know each other. Gap: No intervention targets client-facing staffing, which drives career development in consulting. Gap: No intersectional analysis — gender gap is likely worse for women of color."

**Grade: A** — Two strong catches on accountability and calibration realism, missed client staffing and intersectionality.

#### Steering Feedback

**Criticism:** The accountability architecture request was comprehensive and specific — targets, timelines, consequences, data-restricted region protocols, and governance. The calibration process redesign was practical and well-sequenced. Both produced high-quality, implementable output.

**Grade: A** — Detailed, realistic, and produced the most critical deliverable components.

#### Updated AI Output

**Intervention 4: Equitable Client-Facing Staffing**

| Component | Design |
|---|---|
| Quarterly staffing audit | HR analytics team reviews project assignments by demographic and project tier (Tier 1: marquee clients, largest budgets; Tier 2: mid-market; Tier 3: smaller/routine). Flags when any demographic group is underrepresented in Tier 1 assignments by > 10 percentage points vs their cohort share. |
| Real-time staffing dashboard | Staffing managers see a diversity indicator when building project teams. Not a veto — an awareness prompt: "This project team has no representation from underrepresented groups at this level." |
| Stretch assignment tracker | For each consultant at manager level and above, track number of Tier 1 engagements, client relationship ownership, and revenue attribution. Review quarterly by cohort for demographic patterns. |
| Intervention protocol | If audit reveals skew: staffing manager receives coaching. If pattern persists over 2 quarters: escalation to practice leader. If practice-wide pattern: structural review of staffing process (criteria, who decides, how conflicts are resolved). |
| Client relationship challenge | Address the uncomfortable dimension: when clients request "certain types" of consultants, the firm's policy must be clear and partners must be trained on how to manage these conversations while protecting both client relationship and employee equity. |

**Intersectional Measurement Layer:**

| Data Context | Approach | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Full demographic data available (e.g., US, UK) | Disaggregate all outcome metrics by gender x race/ethnicity, gender x parenting status, race x level | Promotion rate for Black women vs white women vs all women; attrition for parents vs non-parents by gender |
| Limited demographic data (e.g., EU countries) | Proxy measures: inclusion index disaggregated by gender + self-identified dimensions (on opt-in basis); qualitative experience audits with intersectional sampling | Annual focus groups with women in regional offices specifically exploring whether barriers differ by nationality, language, or cultural background |
| No demographic data available | Process compliance metrics only: % of calibration sessions with trained facilitators, % of staffing decisions using equity prompts, qualitative narratives from exit interviews | Regional advisory panels assess whether process compliance is producing observable changes in experience and advancement |

**Updated Barrier Analysis (Intersectional View):**

| Barrier | Gender-only Impact | Intersectional Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Ambiguous evaluation criteria | Women rated lower on "executive presence" | Black and Latina women penalized most severely; mothers rated lower on "commitment" |
| Informal sponsorship | Women receive less sponsorship overall | Women of color receive sponsorship at roughly half the rate of white women |
| Staffing on prestigious projects | Women slightly underrepresented | Women of color underrepresented at 2-3x the rate of white women in Tier 1 staffing |
| Flexible work penalty | Women who take leave penalized | Penalty more severe in offices with traditional cultural norms; fathers who take leave face stigma in some regions |

#### Judging Feedback

**Criticism:** Both catches were excellent and addressed the strategy's most significant blind spots. The client-facing staffing intervention is arguably the highest-leverage point in a consulting firm's equity architecture — it directly controls the career currency (client relationships, revenue attribution) that determines who makes partner. The intersectionality observation correctly identifies that aggregate gender metrics mask compounding disadvantages. One issue the student missed: the strategy does not address how the firm handles the tension between meritocracy narratives ("we promote the best people") and equity goals ("we're reforming the systems that define 'best'"). Without directly confronting this tension, the strategy will face resistance from partners who believe the current system is already meritocratic.

**Better Alternative Judgement:**
> "Gap: No intervention on client-facing staffing. Gap: No intersectional lens. Gap: Strategy doesn't address the meritocracy narrative — partners who believe the current system is fair will resist reforms."

**Grade: A** — Two excellent, high-impact catches. Missed the narrative/resistance dimension.

#### Steering Feedback

**Criticism:** Both corrections were sophisticated and well-specified. The staffing intervention addresses the uncomfortable client preference dimension, which most DEI strategies avoid. The intersectional measurement approach is practical — acknowledging data limitations across jurisdictions while still pushing for disaggregated analysis where possible.

**Grade: A** — Sophisticated, practical, and politically aware corrections.
