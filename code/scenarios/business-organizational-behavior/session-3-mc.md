## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Design an intervention plan for a 12-person cross-functional product development team at a consumer electronics company that has "missed its last three deadlines" and whose members "don't trust each other." The team includes engineers, designers, and marketers spread across offices in San Jose and Shenzhen. The team lead (an engineer) was "promoted from within" six months ago and "some people don't respect her authority." Two senior engineers "dominate every meeting" while others "stay silent." HR has received "a few informal complaints" about the team atmosphere. Deliver a diagnostic framework, a sequence of structured interventions, and success metrics with a 90-day evaluation plan.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** Replace the team lead immediately — she clearly isn't qualified. Disband the team and reassign everyone to different projects.
- **(B)** The trust deficit means people withhold information and avoid vulnerability. The cross-site split likely amplifies power dynamics. The team lead needs both structural empowerment and skill development. Question whether missed deadlines are relational or also structural (scope, process, resources).
- **(C)** The trust deficit manifests as information withholding and blame. Question the nature of HR complaints (friction vs harassment — determines intervention approach). Question whether the team lead wants to stay in the role. The cross-site split amplifies power imbalance (headquarters dominance). Missed deadlines may have structural causes beyond interpersonal dysfunction. The dominant engineers may have been passed over for the lead role.
- **(D)** This is purely a personality conflict — do a Myers-Briggs assessment and rearrange seating.
- **(E)** Send everyone to a team-building retreat with trust falls and rope courses. The problem will resolve itself once people bond socially.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Diagnostic Framework:**
1. Confidential 1:1 interviews with all 12 members
2. Psychological safety survey (Edmondson's 7 items)
3. Relationship mapping (who works with whom, who avoids whom)
4. Process audit of last 3 missed deadlines

**Intervention Sequence:**
- Weeks 1-2: Diagnosis
- Weeks 3-4: Team lead coaching (authority establishment, facilitation skills)
- Weeks 5-6: Facilitated team dialogue (structured format, each person speaks uninterrupted)
- Weeks 7-9: Team co-creates new meeting norms, decision protocols, communication rhythms
- Weeks 10-12: Weekly retrospectives with rotating facilitators

**Success Metrics:**
- Psychological safety: 15% improvement from baseline
- Speaking time balance: Gini coefficient < 0.3
- Next milestone met on time
- Zero new HR complaints in 90 days

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) is solid but incomplete. It correctly identifies the trust deficit's behavioral manifestations, the cross-site power dynamic, the team lead's dual need for empowerment and development, and the structural vs relational question about deadlines. However, option (C) was stronger: it also asks about the nature of HR complaints (which could change the entire intervention from team development to HR investigation), questions whether the team lead wants to remain in the role, and considers whether the dominant engineers were passed over. These are not minor details — they determine whether team-level interventions are even appropriate.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — covers everything in (B) plus the critical questions about complaint severity, team lead commitment, and engineer motivations.

**Grade: B** — Good foundational analysis, missed questions that determine intervention appropriateness.

