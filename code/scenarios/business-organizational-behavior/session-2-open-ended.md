## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Prepare a change management plan for a 120-year-old family-owned insurance company (approximately 8,000 employees, mostly in claims processing and underwriting) that is moving from paper-based workflows to a fully digital platform over "the next two to three years." About 40% of the workforce is "within ten years of retirement" and many have "never used anything beyond email." The new CEO (third-generation family) wants to "modernize without losing the company's personal touch with clients." Two previous technology rollouts "failed badly" and created lasting distrust. Deliver a phased change roadmap, a stakeholder engagement strategy, and a resistance mitigation plan with specific interventions.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The digital platform replaces core workflows — claims intake, processing, underwriting assessment, and policy management. This is not just adding a tool on top of existing processes; it's a fundamental process redesign.**
>
> **Assumption: The two previous failures were technology-driven rollouts that didn't address the human side — training was inadequate, communication was poor, and employees felt the changes were imposed on them.**
>
> **Constraint: 40% near retirement means a large segment may see no personal benefit from learning new systems. Their cooperation depends on different motivators than career advancement.**
>
> **Question: "Personal touch with clients" — I'll assume this means the company values long-term client relationships where employees know clients by name. Digitization risks depersonalizing these interactions.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Phased Change Roadmap (Kotter's 8 Steps):**

| Phase | Kotter Step | Activities | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1: Urgency | Create urgency | Share competitive threat data, client satisfaction trends, industry benchmarks | Months 1-2 |
| 2: Coalition | Build guiding coalition | Recruit influential leaders from claims, underwriting, and IT as change champions | Months 2-3 |
| 3: Vision | Develop vision & strategy | "Digital with a personal touch" — technology enables better client relationships, not replaces them | Months 3-4 |
| 4: Communication | Communicate the vision | Company-wide town halls, manager cascades, intranet, posters | Months 4-6 |
| 5: Empowerment | Remove obstacles | Training programs, updated job descriptions, new performance metrics | Months 6-12 |
| 6: Quick wins | Generate short-term wins | Pilot digital intake in 2 branch offices, celebrate early adopters | Months 6-9 |
| 7: Consolidation | Build on gains | Expand to all offices, refine based on pilot feedback | Months 12-24 |
| 8: Anchoring | Anchor in culture | Update onboarding, reward systems, promote digital-savvy leaders | Months 24-30 |

**Stakeholder Engagement Strategy:**
- Senior leaders: Monthly steering committee meetings
- Middle managers: Bi-weekly change champion network sessions
- Frontline employees: Town halls + small-group Q&A sessions
- IT department: Weekly integration standups
- Clients: Proactive communication about enhanced service

**Resistance Mitigation Plan:**
1. Near-retirement employees: Offer "digital ambassador" roles pairing tech-savvy juniors with experienced staff
2. Previous failure skeptics: Acknowledge past mistakes publicly, show how this effort differs
3. Client-facing staff: Demonstrate that digital tools enhance (not replace) client relationships
4. Union considerations (if applicable): Early engagement and negotiated transition terms

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong moves — identifying the scope as fundamental process redesign rather than tool adoption, inferring the likely causes of past failures, and recognizing the distinct motivational profile of near-retirement workers. The "personal touch" interpretation was also reasonable. However, the student did not ask about the budget or resource constraints — is the company investing EUR 5 million or EUR 50 million? This fundamentally shapes what's feasible. The student also did not question the timeline flexibility — is "two to three years" a hard external deadline (regulatory, competitive) or a CEO aspiration that could shift? Additionally, the student did not ask whether there are union agreements or works council requirements (common in European insurance companies) that constrain how workforce changes can be implemented.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Fundamental process redesign, not tool overlay. Question: What is the budget envelope — this determines whether we can afford dedicated change management resources, extensive training, or only lean interventions? Question: Is the 2-3 year timeline a hard deadline or flexible — regulatory drivers, competitive pressure? Question: Are there union agreements or works council arrangements that constrain workforce changes, role redesign, or mandatory training? Assumption: Past failures were technology-led without adequate human-side change management. Constraint: Near-retirement segment needs distinct engagement approach."

**Grade: A** — Strong diagnosis of the human dynamics, but missed critical feasibility constraints (budget, timeline firmness, labor relations).
