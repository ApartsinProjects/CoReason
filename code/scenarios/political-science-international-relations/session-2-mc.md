## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Prepare a policy evaluation brief assessing whether a hypothetical successor agreement to the JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal) serves U.S. nonproliferation interests. The proposed deal would limit Iran's uranium enrichment to 3.67% for 12 years, allow "enhanced" IAEA inspections, and provide sanctions relief covering "most economic sectors." Iran insists on a "guaranteed" timeline for sanctions removal, while congressional critics call any deal "appeasement." Several Gulf states have signaled they would "pursue their own nuclear options" if the deal is perceived as weak. Deliver a 4-page assessment structured around nonproliferation effectiveness, verification adequacy, regional stability implications, and domestic political feasibility.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** Assume the deal is good because diplomacy is always better than war. Focus the brief on arguing for ratification. Treat Gulf state concerns as bluffing.
- **(B)** Define "enhanced" inspections as beyond the Additional Protocol, estimate breakout timeline under the deal's constraints, note that "most economic sectors" needs specification (Central Bank? Oil exports?), evaluate against the no-deal baseline rather than an ideal deal.
- **(C)** Define "enhanced" inspections precisely. Estimate breakout timeline and test its sensitivity to cheating on individual parameters. Question what happens at the 12-year sunset. Ask about snap-back sanctions mechanism. Evaluate against three alternatives: no deal, stricter deal (likely unachievable), and military option. Map Gulf states' specific proliferation pathways and Israel's red lines.
- **(D)** Assume Iran will cheat regardless, so the agreement is worthless. Focus the brief on making the case for military strikes. Ignore congressional dynamics.
- **(E)** The problem provides enough detail. Just ask the AI to write a balanced 4-page assessment.

**Student selects: (C)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POLICY EVALUATION BRIEF: Proposed Iran Nuclear Agreement**

**I. Nonproliferation Effectiveness**

*Breakout Timeline Analysis:*
- Under agreement: ~12 months (5,060 IR-1 centrifuges, 300 kg LEU stockpile cap, 3.67% limit)
- Without agreement: ~2-3 months (unconstrained centrifuges, growing stockpile, 20%+ enrichment already achieved)
- Sensitivity: Exceeding stockpile to 600 kg reduces breakout to ~8 months; covert IR-6 centrifuges reduce to ~4 months

*Sunset Clause Concern:*
- Year 12+: All enrichment constraints expire. Iran retains full technical base.
- Breakout time post-sunset could shrink to weeks if Iran has expanded capacity.
- Recommendation: Begin successor agreement negotiations no later than year 8.

**II. Verification Adequacy**
- Enhanced inspections: Continuous monitoring at declared sites + challenge inspection mechanism for undeclared sites
- Challenge inspection timeline: 24-60 days (Joint Commission arbitration if Iran objects)
- Detection-to-response window: Challenge inspection (24-60 days) + IAEA analysis (1-2 weeks) + UNSC action (2-4 weeks) = 2-4 months minimum
- Snap-back mechanism: Any JCPOA participant can trigger automatic reimposition of sanctions within 65 days — no veto possible
- Assessment: 12-month breakout minus 2-4 month detection/response = 8-10 month response window — adequate for diplomatic response, tight for military positioning

**III. Regional Stability**
- Saudi Arabia: Has signaled interest in enrichment capability; could pursue Pakistani assistance
- UAE: Nuclear cooperation agreement with U.S. (123 Agreement) constrains enrichment, but political pressure may erode commitment
- Israel: Views any enrichment as unacceptable; retains unilateral military option
- Proposed mitigation: Extended deterrence commitments, regional missile defense, Nuclear Security Dialogue initiative

**IV. Domestic Political Feasibility**
- Format: Executive agreement under INARA (avoids treaty ratification)
- Congressional challenge: Resolution of disapproval likely; must be sustained by presidential veto
- Strategy: Classified briefings on Iran's no-deal trajectory; endorsements from military/intelligence leaders; framing as "constraint vs. no constraint"

**Alternatives Comparison:**

| Criterion | Proposed Deal | No Deal | Military Strike |
|---|---|---|---|
| Breakout time | 12 months | 2-3 months | Set back 2-4 years, then unconstrained |
| Verification | Enhanced + snap-back | Standard IAEA only | None post-strike |
| Regional stability | Managed with assurances | Proliferation cascade risk | Retaliation risk, regional war |
| Domestic feasibility | Contested but viable | Status quo | Deeply divisive |

**Criticism:** Option (C) was the most comprehensive framing available. It correctly pushed for breakout sensitivity analysis, raised the sunset clause, asked about snap-back, demanded evaluation against three alternatives, and mapped regional actors' specific proliferation pathways. This level of specificity produced a significantly more rigorous AI output than options (A), (B), or (E) would have.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** was the best choice.

**Grade: A** — Comprehensive framing that addressed technical, temporal, regional, and domestic dimensions.
