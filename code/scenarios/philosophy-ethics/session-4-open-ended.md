## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> A government bioethics commission must draft policy recommendations on whether to permit human germline gene editing for heritable genetic conditions. The technology can "correct" mutations causing diseases like sickle cell anemia and cystic fibrosis, but could also be used for "enhancements" like increased muscle mass or cognitive traits. A clinical trial abroad has already produced "a few" gene-edited babies. Disability rights advocates say the technology "devalues disabled lives." Religious groups have "mixed views." The commission needs a 5-page policy recommendation document with an ethical framework section, a risk-benefit analysis, specific regulatory proposals, and a public engagement plan.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The policy addresses germline editing only (heritable changes), not somatic gene therapy (non-heritable), since germline editing affects future generations who cannot consent.**
>
> **Assumption: There is a morally meaningful distinction between therapy (correcting disease-causing mutations) and enhancement (improving traits beyond normal range), even though the boundary is contested.**
>
> **Question: The problem says disability rights advocates claim the technology "devalues disabled lives" — this is a substantive moral argument, not just a stakeholder concern. The policy must engage with it philosophically, not merely "note" it.**
>
> **Missing: No information about the current scientific consensus on safety — off-target effects, mosaicism, long-term unknowns. The ethical permissibility depends partly on the risk level.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POLICY RECOMMENDATION: HUMAN GERMLINE GENE EDITING**

**1. Ethical Framework:**
The commission adopts a principlist approach (Beauchamp & Childress):
- **Autonomy:** Future persons cannot consent to germline modifications. Parental autonomy in reproductive decisions is relevant but not unlimited.
- **Beneficence:** Eliminating heritable disease reduces suffering.
- **Non-maleficence:** Off-target effects and unknown long-term risks may cause harm.
- **Justice:** If gene editing is expensive, it may be available only to wealthy families, creating genetic inequality.

**2. Therapy vs Enhancement Distinction:**
The commission recommends permitting gene editing only for conditions classified as "serious genetic disorders" by established medical criteria (e.g., conditions causing significant suffering, disability, or premature death). Enhancement applications (cosmetic, cognitive, athletic) are prohibited.

**3. Risk-Benefit Analysis:**
- Benefits: Elimination of heritable disease, reduced healthcare burden, reduced suffering
- Risks: Off-target genetic effects, unknown multi-generational consequences, social pressure to edit, slippery slope toward enhancement

**4. Regulatory Proposals:**
- Establish a national gene editing oversight board
- Require clinical trial approval for any germline intervention
- Maintain a registry of all germline-edited individuals for long-term follow-up
- Prohibit enhancement applications
- Review the therapy/enhancement boundary every 5 years

**5. Public Engagement Plan:**
- National consultation process including disability rights organizations, religious groups, patient advocacy groups, and scientific bodies
- Citizens' assemblies in major cities
- Public report summarizing views and how they influenced the final policy

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student made several strong framing choices: distinguishing germline from somatic editing, flagging the therapy/enhancement boundary as contested, and insisting that the disability rights argument be engaged with philosophically rather than merely noted. However, the student has a significant gap: the framing does not question whose concept of "disease" and "normal range" defines the therapy/enhancement boundary. This boundary is not a neutral medical fact — it reflects cultural and historical judgments about which bodies and minds are "normal." Disability rights scholars (e.g., the social model of disability) argue that many conditions classified as diseases are disabilities only because society fails to accommodate them. The student also did not question the intergenerational consent problem more deeply: if future persons cannot consent, does this make germline editing impermissible, or does the same logic apply to all parental decisions that shape children's lives (education, diet, environment)?

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: Germline vs somatic distinction is critical — germline affects non-consenting future persons. Question: The therapy/enhancement boundary presupposes a concept of 'normal' that may itself be culturally constructed. Who defines which conditions are diseases? The social model of disability challenges medical model assumptions. Question: The intergenerational consent objection, if applied consistently, would prohibit many parental decisions — need to address why germline editing is different. Missing: Scientific safety data, existing international regulatory frameworks (Oviedo Convention, etc.), and how other jurisdictions have handled this."

**Grade: B** — Strong on several key distinctions but missed the constructed nature of the therapy/enhancement boundary and the consistency test for the consent objection.

**POLICY RECOMMENDATION (Revised)**

**1. Ethical Framework (Expanded):**

*(Principlist framework retained, plus:)*

**The Expressivist Objection:**
Disability rights scholars argue that germline editing for conditions like deafness, dwarfism, or certain neurodivergent conditions expresses a negative evaluative judgment about people who currently live with those conditions. By classifying these traits as defects to be eliminated, society communicates that lives lived with these conditions are less valuable — that these people should not have existed, or that the world would be better without people like them.

*Response:* Proponents distinguish between the trait and the person. Eliminating sickle cell disease does not imply that people with sickle cell disease have lives not worth living — it reflects a judgment that the suffering caused by the disease is bad, not that the people are bad. Parents who would choose gene editing for a child are not rejecting their existing children.

*Counter-response:* The expressivist argues this distinction is unstable in practice. When a society systematically selects against certain conditions, the social meaning of having that condition changes. People with the condition become "those who slipped through," which shapes how they are treated, funded, and valued. The policy must grapple with this social-meaning dimension, not just the individual-choice dimension.

*Policy implication:* Any approved list of conditions eligible for germline editing must be developed with the direct involvement of disability rights advocates and people living with the conditions in question, not by medical geneticists alone.

**2. The Therapy/Enhancement Boundary — A Process, Not a Line:**

The commission recognizes that "serious genetic disorder" cannot be defined purely by medical criteria. Whether a condition counts as a disorder depends on:
- Medical factors: Does the condition cause physical suffering or premature death?
- Social factors: Is the "disability" partly or wholly a product of social arrangements that could be changed?
- Subjective factors: How do people living with the condition describe their own quality of life?

Rather than a fixed list, the commission proposes a **Condition Review Board** composed of geneticists, bioethicists, disability rights advocates, and patient representatives. This board will evaluate proposed conditions case by case, publish reasoning, and be subject to judicial review. The board's composition must include at least 30% members who are themselves people with disabilities.

#### Updated AI Output

**POLICY RECOMMENDATION (Final Revision)**

**3. Risk-Benefit Analysis (Expanded):**

**Systemic Social Risks:**
- **Genetic stratification:** If germline editing is expensive and market-driven, it risks creating a biological dimension to existing socioeconomic inequality. Children of wealthy families would have both social advantages and engineered biological advantages, compounding intergenerational injustice.
- **Eugenic pressure:** As gene editing becomes normalized, parents may face social pressure to edit, turning a voluntary choice into a de facto expectation. "Why didn't you fix that?" becomes a socially acceptable question.
- **Commodification of reproduction:** Treating genetic traits as consumer choices transforms reproduction into a marketplace, which may erode the unconditional acceptance that characterizes the parent-child relationship.

*Justice analysis:* Rawlsian justice would require that genetic editing, if permitted, be available to all or prohibited entirely — the difference principle does not justify a technology that systematically advantages the already-advantaged. A Rawlsian policy would either publicly fund gene editing as a universal healthcare provision or prohibit it until universal access is feasible.

**International Dimension:**
- **Reproductive tourism:** Citizens may travel to jurisdictions where gene editing is legal. A domestic prohibition cannot fully prevent this. The commission recommends: (a) the domestic prohibition applies to procedures performed on UK citizens regardless of jurisdiction (recognizing enforcement challenges), (b) gene-edited persons born abroad are not discriminated against, (c) the UK actively pursues international regulatory harmonization.
- **Regulatory coordination:** The commission recommends engaging with WHO, UNESCO, and EU partners to develop minimum international standards, similar to the Oviedo Convention approach.

For a topic this morally contested, the engagement plan should specify how conflicting moral views are weighted in the final policy — is it majority rule, consensus-seeking, or expert-weighted deliberation?
