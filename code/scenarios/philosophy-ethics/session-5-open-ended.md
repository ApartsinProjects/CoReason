### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> An international NGO coalition wants to propose a framework for climate reparations at an upcoming UN summit. Developing nations bear "disproportionate climate impacts" but some are now "major emitters themselves." Small island states face "existential threats" from rising seas. The coalition wants a position paper (4-5 pages) that argues for a specific reparations model, defines who owes what to whom, proposes a funding mechanism, and addresses the objection that "current generations shouldn't pay for past generations' emissions." The paper should be "philosophically rigorous but politically viable."

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POSITION PAPER: A FRAMEWORK FOR CLIMATE REPARATIONS**

**1. The Moral Case for Climate Reparations:**
Climate reparations are grounded in corrective justice: wealthy industrialized nations caused disproportionate harm through historical greenhouse gas emissions, and the resulting climate impacts fall disproportionately on nations that contributed least to the problem.

Three independent moral grounds support the claim:
- **Polluter pays:** Those who caused the harm should bear the cost of remediation.
- **Beneficiary principle:** Current generations in wealthy nations enjoy standards of living built on carbon-intensive industrialization. Even if they did not personally emit, they are the beneficiaries of a system that created the harm.
- **Capacity principle:** Regardless of historical responsibility, wealthy nations have the capacity to fund adaptation, and severe need combined with capacity creates an obligation.

**2. Who Owes What to Whom:**
- **Debtors:** Nations whose cumulative historical emissions exceed their per-capita share of the global carbon budget.
- **Creditors:** Nations whose per-capita emissions are below their share but who bear disproportionate climate impacts.
- **Quantum:** Calculated based on the social cost of carbon applied to excess historical emissions, adjusted for current capacity to pay.

**3. Responding to the Intergenerational Objection:**
"Current generations shouldn't pay for past emissions."
*Response:* Current generations in wealthy nations are not innocent bystanders to historical emissions. They are the direct beneficiaries of the wealth, infrastructure, and institutions built by carbon-intensive industrialization. The beneficiary principle holds that those who profit from injustice acquire an obligation to remedy it, even if they did not commit the injustice themselves. Analogy: inheriting a house built with stolen materials creates an obligation to the theft's victim, even if the inheritor did not steal.

**4. Funding Mechanism:**
- A global climate reparations fund financed by contributions based on excess cumulative emissions x GDP per capita.
- Disbursement prioritized by climate vulnerability index (small island states first).
- Governed by a board with equal representation from debtor and creditor nations.

**5. Political Viability:**
- Frame as "climate investment" rather than "reparations" for diplomatic contexts.
- Build on existing mechanisms (Green Climate Fund, Loss and Damage fund).
- Phase in contributions over 20 years to reduce political resistance.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** This was a strong framing. The student correctly identified the corrective justice foundation, presented three independent moral grounds for the claim, anticipated the central philosophical objection with multiple possible responses, and flagged the tension between philosophical rigor and political viability. The identification of the unit-of-analysis question (nations vs peoples vs generations) was particularly perceptive. However, the student did not question one important conceptual issue: does the "reparations" frame itself presuppose a particular moral relationship (debtor-creditor) that may not apply to climate change? Climate change is not a bilateral wrong like slavery or colonialism — it is a diffuse, cumulative, and partly unknowing process. Some philosophers (e.g., Caney) argue that a distributive justice frame (fair allocation of costs) is more appropriate than a corrective justice frame (reparations for a wrong). The student's framing assumed the corrective justice frame without examining whether it is the right one.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> *(Student's framing, plus:)* "Question: Is 'reparations' the right moral frame? Corrective justice assumes a specific wrongdoer and victim, but climate change involves diffuse, cumulative emissions with many contributors acting over centuries. A distributive justice frame (fair allocation of adaptation costs based on capacity and benefit) might be more philosophically defensible and politically tractable. The choice of frame changes the entire argument structure."

**Grade: A** — Unusually strong framing with one significant conceptual gap regarding the justice frame.

**POSITION PAPER (Revised)**

**3. Responding to the Intergenerational Objection (Strengthened):**

The inherited-house analogy has limits. Historical emissions were legal when they occurred, widely diffused across populations, and their climatic effects were not understood for much of the relevant period. This weakens the strongest version of the corrective justice claim — if past emitters did not know they were causing harm, the analogy to theft is strained.

Three responses:

*First,* the knowledge-ignorance defense has a time limit. By 1990 at the latest (IPCC First Assessment Report), the causal link between emissions and climate harm was scientifically established. Emissions since 1990 cannot be excused by ignorance. Since wealthy nations have continued to emit disproportionately for 35+ years after this knowledge threshold, the ongoing-emissions argument is sufficient on its own: the harm is not merely historical.

*Second,* even for pre-1990 emissions, the beneficiary principle does not require that the original emitters knew they were causing harm. If you discover that the wealth you inherited was acquired through exploitation (even exploitation that was legal at the time), the discovery creates an obligation. The moral claim is forward-looking: now that we know, what do we owe?

*Third,* the corrective justice frame can be supplemented by a distributive justice frame. Even setting aside historical fault entirely, the current distribution of climate costs is unjust: those who can least afford adaptation bear the greatest impacts. A distributive justice argument based on capacity and need does not depend on the corrective justice claim at all — it is an independent ground.

**2a. Intra-National Distribution:**
The nation-to-nation framework is a pragmatic necessity for international negotiations, but it conceals important moral complexity:
- **Within debtor nations:** Funding should be raised progressively — a carbon levy on high-emission industries and wealth taxes on those who benefited most from carbon-intensive development, not regressive taxes that burden domestic vulnerable populations.
- **Within creditor nations:** Disbursement should prioritize the most climate-vulnerable communities and populations, not flow to national governments that may not distribute equitably.
- **Design implication:** The reparations fund's governance should include mechanisms for sub-national allocation and civil society oversight in both debtor and creditor nations.

#### Updated AI Output

**POSITION PAPER (Final Revision)**

**4a. Funding Formula — Addressing Double-Weighting:**
The formula (excess emissions x GDP per capita) intentionally weights contributions toward wealthier high-emitting nations. Critics may argue this double-counts: GDP per capita is partly a product of the emissions being penalized, so the formula penalizes both the cause (emissions) and the effect (wealth).

*Our position:* This double-weighting is a justified feature, not an error. The moral claim is that both the emissions and the wealth derived from them create obligation. The beneficiary principle specifically holds that enjoying the fruits of unjust enrichment creates a duty of restitution proportional to the benefit received. A formula that weighted only emissions without considering capacity would impose equal burdens on nations with vastly different abilities to pay, which would itself be unjust.

However, to prevent extreme results, the formula should include a cap: no nation's annual contribution exceeds a specified percentage of GDP (we propose 2%), ensuring the reparations framework does not itself create economic injustice.

**4b. Enforcement and Participation Incentives:**
No international mechanism can compel sovereign nations to participate. The paper proposes a multi-layered incentive structure:
- **Linkage:** Climate reparations compliance as a condition for favorable trade terms, similar to labor and environmental standards in modern trade agreements.
- **Reputational costs:** Public ranking of nations by compliance, creating diplomatic pressure.
- **Reciprocity:** Non-participating nations excluded from reparations fund benefits (e.g., green technology transfer, disaster relief coordination).
- **Graduated entry:** Allow phased participation to reduce political barriers.

*Limitation:* These mechanisms create pressure but not compulsion. The paper acknowledges that the framework depends partly on political will and moral persuasion — a limitation shared by all international justice mechanisms, from the ICC to the Paris Agreement.
