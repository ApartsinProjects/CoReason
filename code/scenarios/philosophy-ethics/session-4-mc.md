## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> A government bioethics commission must draft policy recommendations on whether to permit human germline gene editing for heritable genetic conditions. The technology can "correct" mutations causing diseases like sickle cell anemia and cystic fibrosis, but could also be used for "enhancements" like increased muscle mass or cognitive traits. A clinical trial abroad has already produced "a few" gene-edited babies. Disability rights advocates say the technology "devalues disabled lives." Religious groups have "mixed views." The commission needs a 5-page policy recommendation document with an ethical framework section, a risk-benefit analysis, specific regulatory proposals, and a public engagement plan.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and clarifications should you establish?

- **(A)** Assume the technology is safe and focus entirely on whether enhancement should be permitted. The therapy question is already settled — of course we should cure genetic diseases.
- **(B)** Distinguish germline from somatic editing. Recognize the therapy/enhancement boundary as contested. Note that the disability rights argument requires philosophical engagement. Identify missing safety data.
- **(C)** Distinguish germline from somatic editing. Question the therapy/enhancement boundary — whose concept of "disease" and "normal" defines it? The social model of disability challenges medical classifications. Engage the expressivist objection as a moral argument, not a stakeholder concern. Question whether the intergenerational consent objection applies uniquely to gene editing or to all parental decisions. Note missing data: safety evidence, international regulatory landscape, views of people living with the conditions.
- **(D)** Frame this as a religious question — the key issue is whether humans should "play God." Scientific and philosophical frameworks are secondary.
- **(E)** Assume a libertarian framework — parents should be free to make any genetic choice for their children. The commission's role is to ensure informed consent, not restrict options.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**POLICY RECOMMENDATION: HUMAN GERMLINE GENE EDITING**

**Ethical Framework:** Principlist approach (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice). Future persons cannot consent to germline changes.

**Therapy/Enhancement:** Permit editing for "serious genetic disorders" per established medical criteria. Prohibit enhancements.

**Risk-Benefit:** Benefits — reduced disease, reduced suffering. Risks — off-target effects, unknown long-term consequences, slippery slope.

**Regulatory Proposals:** National oversight board, clinical trial requirements, registry, enhancement prohibition, 5-year boundary reviews.

**Public Engagement:** National consultation, citizens' assemblies, public report.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (B) covers essential ground — the germline/somatic distinction, the contested boundary, the disability argument, and safety gaps. However, option (C) was significantly stronger. It questions whose concept of "disease" defines the therapy/enhancement boundary, engaging the social model of disability rather than accepting medical classification as neutral. It also applies a consistency test to the intergenerational consent objection (does it apply to all parental decisions?) and specifies that the views of people living with the conditions are missing data. These are not refinements — they challenge the foundations of the analysis.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — questions the concepts embedded in the problem rather than accepting them.

**Grade: B** — Solid but accepted key concepts uncritically.
