## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> A mid-level engineer at an automotive company discovers that the emissions control software has a "defeat device" that produces lower emissions readings during regulatory testing than in real-world driving. The company's vehicles are "significantly" exceeding legal pollution limits on the road. The engineer's manager says the issue is "being handled internally" and warns against "creating problems." The engineer has a family, a mortgage, and signed a non-disclosure agreement. Prepare a structured ethical analysis document (3-4 pages) that lays out the engineer's options, evaluates each against multiple ethical frameworks, recommends a course of action, and includes a section on what institutional safeguards should exist to prevent this situation.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and clarifications should you establish?

- **(A)** Assume the manager is handling it correctly. The engineer should trust the chain of command. The NDA settles the question — the engineer cannot disclose anything.
- **(B)** Assume intentional fraud, direct evidence available, NDA may not cover illegal activity. Use utilitarianism, Kant, and virtue ethics as frameworks. Focus on the individual engineer's decision.
- **(C)** Assume intentional fraud, direct evidence available. Distinguish between individual moral responsibility and institutional/structural responsibility. Include professional engineering codes of ethics (role morality) alongside standard frameworks. Question whether the manager's warning signals compromised internal channels. Flag that the NDA's legal enforceability and moral weight are separate questions. Note missing information: whistleblower legal protections, history of internal dissent treatment.
- **(D)** Frame this as purely a legal question — what does the law require? Ethical analysis is secondary to contractual and regulatory obligations.
- **(E)** The engineer should weigh personal financial consequences (mortgage, family) against abstract moral duties. Since the personal costs are concrete and the moral duties are uncertain, silence is justifiable.

**Student selects: (C)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**ETHICAL ANALYSIS: WHISTLEBLOWER DECISION**

**Options:** Remain silent, escalate internally, report to regulator, public disclosure.

**Framework Analysis:** Developed Kantian argument (universalizability of silence is self-defeating; humanity formula — affected communities treated as means). Consequentialist analysis weighing health harm against personal and economic costs. Virtue ethics distinguishing courage from recklessness. Professional engineering code: paramount duty to public safety.

**Institutional Analysis:** Root cause diagnosis — diffused responsibility, normalization of deviance, managerial pressure without accountability. Targeted safeguards: individual sign-off requirements, independent testing authority, ethical culture audits, protected escalation channels.

**NDA Analysis:** Legal enforceability questionable for fraud coverage; moral weight diminished because promise extracted under deceptive conditions.

**Recommendation:** Document evidence, secure legal counsel, report to regulator directly (internal channels likely compromised given manager's warning). Internal escalation above manager permissible as parallel step.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (C) was the strongest available choice and the student selected it. It correctly distinguishes individual from institutional responsibility, includes professional role morality, identifies the compromised-channel signal, and separates the NDA's legal and moral dimensions. This demonstrates mature ethical framing that goes well beyond applying standard textbook frameworks.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** was the best choice.

**Grade: A** — Comprehensive framing with appropriate sophistication for the problem.
