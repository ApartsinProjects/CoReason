> Prepare a legal memorandum for a student expelled from a public university after being found responsible for academic misconduct through the university's internal disciplinary process. The student was notified of the charges "two days before" the hearing, was not permitted to have an attorney present, could not cross-examine witnesses, and the hearing panel included a professor who had filed the original complaint. The university says its process "complies with its handbook" and that academic misconduct hearings are "not criminal proceedings." The student wants to challenge the expulsion in federal court. Produce the memorandum with a procedural due process analysis, the legal standard, and a recommended litigation strategy. The student's family says the university was "out to get him" and wants to include a substantive due process claim as well.

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**LEGAL MEMORANDUM**

**RE:** Due Process Challenge to University Expulsion for Academic Misconduct

**I. Procedural Due Process Analysis**

*A. Protected Interest*
The student holds a protected property interest in continued enrollment at a public university. Under Goss v. Lopez (1975), students facing suspension are entitled to due process protections. Expulsion, as the most severe academic sanction, triggers enhanced procedural protections.

*B. Mathews v. Eldridge Balancing Test*

Factor 1 — Private Interest: The student's interest in continued enrollment is substantial. Expulsion carries severe consequences: loss of educational investment, damage to professional prospects, and stigma that may affect future academic opportunities.

Factor 2 — Risk of Erroneous Deprivation: The current procedures create multiple risks of error:
- Two days' notice is insufficient for the student to prepare a defense, gather evidence, or consult counsel.
- Exclusion of legal counsel deprives the student of assistance in navigating a complex quasi-judicial proceeding.
- Prohibition on cross-examination prevents the student from testing the reliability and credibility of adverse witnesses.
- The complaining professor's presence on the hearing panel creates a structural conflict of interest that undermines impartiality.

Factor 3 — Government Interest: The university has legitimate interests in efficient resolution of academic misconduct and in maintaining academic standards. However, the additional procedural protections sought — reasonable notice, counsel, cross-examination, impartial panel — impose modest administrative costs that are justified by the severity of the sanction.

*C. Minimum Procedural Requirements*
Under Goss and its progeny, the student was entitled to:
1. Adequate notice of charges and evidence (two days was likely insufficient for expulsion)
2. A meaningful opportunity to be heard (compromised by lack of counsel)
3. An impartial decision-maker (violated by the complaining professor's panel membership)
4. An opportunity to confront adverse evidence (denied by cross-examination prohibition)

*D. Substantive Due Process*
The student's substantive due process claim is weak. Education is not a fundamental right under the federal Constitution (San Antonio v. Rodriguez). However, under County of Sacramento v. Lewis, executive action that "shocks the conscience" violates substantive due process. If the student can demonstrate that the university acted with deliberate bias — using the disciplinary process as a pretext for retaliation — this standard may be met. The evidentiary burden is high.

**II. Recommended Litigation Strategy**
1. File under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 for deprivation of constitutional rights under color of state law.
2. Seek preliminary injunction to restore enrollment pending resolution.
3. Focus discovery on panel communications and prior relationship between the complaining professor and the student.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** The student's framing was strong and well-organized. Correctly identifying the state action question as resolved (public university), the property interest under Goss, and the Mathews v. Eldridge framework was exactly the right analytical path. The honest assessment of the substantive due process claim (difficult but potentially viable under a "shocks the conscience" standard) showed good judgment. However, the student did not address several important dimensions. First, the student did not distinguish between academic and disciplinary proceedings — the Supreme Court in Board of Curators v. Horowitz (1978) held that academic decisions (grades, degree requirements) receive substantial deference, while disciplinary decisions (misconduct charges) require more formal procedures. The characterization of this case as "academic misconduct" creates ambiguity about which standard applies. Second, the student did not consider whether the student also has a liberty interest at stake — if the expulsion record stigmatizes the student in a way that forecloses future educational or employment opportunities, the liberty interest analysis under Paul v. Davis and Siegert v. Gilley becomes relevant. Third, the student did not flag whether exhaustion of administrative remedies is required before filing in federal court.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "Assumption: State action established (public university). Property interest in enrollment under Goss. Question: Is this an 'academic' or 'disciplinary' proceeding under Horowitz? The distinction affects the level of deference the court will give. Question: Is there also a liberty interest — does the expulsion stigmatize the student, foreclosing future opportunities (stigma-plus test under Paul v. Davis)? Constraint: Apply Mathews balancing test to each procedural deficiency. Question: Must administrative remedies be exhausted before federal court filing? The substantive due process claim is weak absent fundamental right (San Antonio v. Rodriguez) but may proceed under 'shocks the conscience' if retaliatory intent is shown."

**Grade: A** — Strong core analysis but missed the academic/disciplinary distinction, liberty interest, and exhaustion questions.

*Cross-examination:* The right to cross-examine adverse witnesses is not automatic in all administrative proceedings. However, under Goldberg v. All three conditions are met here. The academic misconduct charge depends on witness testimony about what occurred — the complaining professor's account versus the student's. *C. Board of Curators v. Horowitz Distinction*

But Horowitz drew a clear line between academic decisions (evaluations of academic performance, which require only "careful and deliberate" assessment) and disciplinary decisions (charges of misconduct requiring formal hearing procedures). Despite the label "academic misconduct," this proceeding is functionally disciplinary: it involves a specific charge of rule-violating conduct, witnesses, a hearing panel, and a sanction imposed for behavior rather than for inadequate academic performance. Under the Horowitz framework, the more formal procedural protections of Goss v. Lopez and Mathews v. Eldridge apply.

**I. Procedural Due Process (Further Revised)**

The complaining professor's membership on the hearing panel violates the due process requirement of impartial adjudication. Under Withrow v.
