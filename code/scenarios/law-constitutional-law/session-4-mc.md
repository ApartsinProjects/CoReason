## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Write a policy analysis and legal memorandum for a state governor's legal counsel assessing whether the state's newly enacted cannabis licensing and regulatory framework can survive a federal preemption challenge. The state has legalized commercial cannabis cultivation, processing, and retail sale with a "comprehensive regulatory scheme." Federal law still classifies cannabis as a Schedule I controlled substance. The U.S. Attorney for the district has made "no public statements" about enforcement priorities. Several state-licensed businesses have been denied banking services because banks fear federal prosecution. The governor wants to know "how vulnerable" the state program is and what "protective measures" the state can adopt.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates the memorandum?

- **(A)** Assume cannabis is already effectively legal because many states have legalized it. The federal law is outdated and will not be enforced. Focus the memo on business licensing details.
- **(B)** Frame as a Supremacy Clause/preemption analysis. Identify the three preemption types (express, field, conflict). Note the CSA savings clause. Flag anti-commandeering as a defense. Observe that the banking problem reflects federal/state tension.
- **(C)** Frame as Supremacy Clause analysis. Distinguish express, field, and conflict preemption with the savings clause (§ 903) as textual anchor. Question whether the state's "comprehensive regulatory scheme" crosses from decriminalization into facilitation. Analyze anti-commandeering (Printz, Murphy) as constitutional defense. Flag Gonzales v. Raich Commerce Clause implications. Consider federal spending conditions as a separate vulnerability pathway. Question whether federal enforcement posture affects the legal (not just practical) analysis.
- **(D)** Focus entirely on the Tenth Amendment — states have reserved power to regulate cannabis within their borders. Federal law is unconstitutional as applied.
- **(E)** Assume the memo should recommend abandoning the cannabis program because federal preemption is inevitable.

**Student selects: (B)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**MEMORANDUM FOR THE GOVERNOR'S LEGAL COUNSEL**

**II. Preemption Analysis**
*A. Express Preemption:* CSA has no express preemption clause for state cannabis laws. Savings clause (§ 903) limits preemption to positive conflicts.
*B. Field Preemption:* Unlikely — savings clause demonstrates Congress did not intend to occupy the field.
*C. Conflict Preemption:* Strongest federal theory. State framework authorizes conduct CSA prohibits. However, most courts have rejected preemption because state law does not require CSA violations.
*D. Protective Measures:* Keep framework permissive; include federal law disclaimers; establish state banking program; monitor enforcement posture.

Raich as strengthening the federal position, considered federal spending conditions as an entirely separate vulnerability, and asked whether enforcement posture has legal significance beyond practical import. These additional dimensions are essential for a complete analysis that the governor can rely on.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(C)** — adds the decriminalization/facilitation distinction, Raich, spending power, and enforcement posture analysis.

**Grade: B** — Correct framework identification but missed several dimensions needed for completeness.

#### Updated AI Output

**II. Preemption Analysis (Revised)**

*C. Conflict Preemption (Expanded):*
Courts distinguish decriminalization (merely removing state penalties — not preempted) from affirmative facilitation (licensing and regulating — more vulnerable). The state's comprehensive framework falls between these poles. Emerald Steel held that requiring accommodation of marijuana use was preempted because the state law required conduct the CSA prohibited. The state's framework does not require participation — the strongest anti-preemption argument.

*D. Anti-Commandeering Defense:*
Under Printz and Murphy v. NCAA, the federal government cannot commandeer state legislatures. The state program can continue as a matter of state law; the federal government cannot enjoin the licensing apparatus; but individual participants remain subject to federal prosecution.

*A. Savings Clause as Textual Anchor (Revised):*
