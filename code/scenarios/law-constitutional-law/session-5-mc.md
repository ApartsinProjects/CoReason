## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Prepare a legal memorandum for a student expelled from a public university after being found responsible for academic misconduct through the university's internal disciplinary process. The student was notified of the charges "two days before" the hearing, was not permitted to have an attorney present, could not cross-examine witnesses, and the hearing panel included a professor who had filed the original complaint. The university says its process "complies with its handbook" and that academic misconduct hearings are "not criminal proceedings." The student wants to challenge the expulsion in federal court. Produce the memorandum with a procedural due process analysis, the legal standard, and a recommended litigation strategy. The student's family says the university was "out to get him" and wants to include a substantive due process claim as well.

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**LEGAL MEMORANDUM**

**I. Protected Interests**
*Property:* Under Goss, enrollment at a public university is a protected property interest.
*Liberty:* Under the stigma-plus test (Paul v. Davis), the academic dishonesty finding, if disclosed, forecloses future educational and professional opportunities.

**II. Horowitz Distinction**
Despite the "academic misconduct" label, this is a disciplinary proceeding — specific charges, hearing panel, sanction for conduct. Under Horowitz, more formal procedures apply than for purely academic evaluations.

**III. Mathews Analysis**
- Notice: Two days insufficient for expulsion-level sanction.
- Counsel: Exclusion denies assistance in complex quasi-judicial proceeding.
- Cross-examination: Not automatic (Mathews-dependent), but required here because charges depend on witness credibility.
- Impartiality: Under Withrow v. Larkin, complaining professor as panelist creates disqualifying conflict — accuser as judge exceeds institutional role combination.

**IV. Substantive Due Process**
Weak claim. Education not a fundamental right (San Antonio v. Rodriguez). But "shocks the conscience" standard (County of Sacramento v. Lewis) may apply if retaliatory intent is demonstrated.

**V. Litigation Strategy**
File § 1983 action. Seek preliminary injunction (Winter factors). Discovery targeted at panel communications and prior relationship.

**III. Mathews Analysis (Expanded)**

*Cross-Examination (Revised):*
Under Goldberg v. Kelly and the Mathews framework, cross-examination is constitutionally required when three conditions converge: (1) the factual issues depend on witness credibility (here, the professor's account of the alleged misconduct versus the student's denial); (2) the consequences of erroneous determination are severe (expulsion from a university); and (3) no adequate alternative means exist to test the adverse evidence (written submissions cannot probe credibility the way cross-examination can). This case is distinguished from Mathews itself, where disability benefits determinations turned on documentary medical records rather than testimonial credibility.

*Impartiality (Revised):*
Under Withrow v. Larkin, combining investigative and adjudicative functions in the same body creates a constitutional concern. But the present case is categorically more problematic: the professor is not merely an institutional actor performing dual roles — the professor is the individual who initiated the complaint and has a personal interest in its vindication. This transforms the bias from institutional (the concern in Withrow) to personal. An accuser who sits as judge has a direct, personal stake in the outcome. This structural bias should be treated as per se disqualifying without requiring a showing of actual prejudice.

*Stigma-Plus (Revised):*
The academic dishonesty finding forecloses specific, tangible opportunities: (1) graduate school applications universally ask about academic misconduct findings; (2) state bar character and fitness examinations inquire about academic disciplinary history; (3) professional licensing in medicine, nursing, finance, and education requires disclosure of misconduct findings. The stigma is not merely reputational — it creates concrete, measurable barriers to future advancement.

**V. Litigation Strategy (Expanded)**

*Preliminary Injunction Under Winter v. NRDC:*
1. *Likelihood of success:* Strong — the panel bias (accuser as judge) is a per se due process violation.
2. *Irreparable harm:* Loss of a semester of education, degree timeline disruption, and ongoing reputational harm from the misconduct finding — none compensable by money damages.
3. *Balance of equities:* Student faces career-altering consequences; university faces minimal harm from readmission pending resolution with appropriate monitoring conditions.
4. *Public interest:* Procedural fairness in public university proceedings serves institutional accountability and protects all students.

#### Updated AI Output

**VI. Anticipated University Defenses**

*A. Horowitz Academic Deference*
The university will argue that academic misconduct proceedings are academic decisions entitled to substantial deference under Horowitz. Counter: As analyzed above, this proceeding is functionally disciplinary — it involves specific behavioral charges, an adversarial hearing, and a punitive sanction. Horowitz deference applies to evaluative academic judgments (grades, degree requirements), not to adjudications of alleged rule violations.

*B. Informal Process Sufficiency*
The university will argue that disciplinary proceedings are "not criminal" and require only "informal give-and-take" per Goss. Counter: Goss's "informal" standard applies to short suspensions (10 days or less). For expulsion — the most severe sanction available — the Mathews balancing requires substantially more procedural protection. The factual complexity (credibility disputes, 100+ page student handbook procedures) and severity of consequences justify formal hearing procedures.

*C. Qualified Immunity*
Individual defendants (hearing panel members, administrators) will assert qualified immunity. Counter: The procedural requirements of Goss (notice, hearing, impartiality) have been clearly established since 1975. The right to an impartial decision-maker is so fundamental that no reasonable official could believe that allowing the accuser to serve as judge satisfies due process. Qualified immunity should not apply.

*D. Exhaustion*
The university may argue that the student failed to exhaust internal appeal procedures. Counter: Under Patsy v. Board of Regents of Florida (1982), exhaustion of state administrative remedies is not required before filing a § 1983 action in federal court. This is a categorical rule, not a discretionary one.
