## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a comprehensive AI-use policy and accompanying pedagogical framework for a 200-level undergraduate "Business Communication" course. The instructor is "not anti-AI" but worries that students are "submitting ChatGPT output as their own work" and "not learning to write." The course requires four major writing assignments (memo, report, proposal, presentation script). The university has a generic AI policy that says "use is permitted with disclosure" but the instructor finds this "too vague to be useful." Some students say AI tools help them "overcome writer's block" while others admit to "just prompting and submitting." Deliver a course-specific AI policy document, a revised assignment design for each of the four assignments that integrates AI use productively, a student-facing guide explaining permitted and prohibited uses with examples, and an assessment rubric that can distinguish AI-assisted work from AI-generated work.

### Framing Options Presented

Which assumptions and constraints should you establish before the AI generates a solution?

- **(A)** Ban all AI use in the course — the only way to ensure students learn to write is to prohibit technology entirely.
- **(B)** Adopt the university's generic "permitted with disclosure" policy as-is. Design assignments as usual and add a disclosure checkbox.
- **(C)** Define AI permissions differently for each assignment based on its learning objectives. Require process documentation (drafts, revisions). Note that AI-detection tools are unreliable. Acknowledge the equity gap between paid and free AI tool access.
- **(D)** Define AI permissions per assignment based on learning outcomes, require verifiable process evidence (not just self-reported AI Use Statements), dismiss AI-detection tools as unreliable and biased against non-native English speakers, address the equity gap in AI tool access, plan for how students will learn and practice the policy before graded work, and consider how the policy will be updated as AI tools evolve.
- **(E)** Require students to use AI for every assignment to prepare them for the modern workplace — resistance to AI is futile.

**Student selects: (C)**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Course AI Policy:**

*Core Principles:*
1. Transparency: AI Use Statement appended to each assignment
2. Process Over Product: grades reflect thinking and revision, not just final quality
3. Differentiated Use: permissions vary by assignment and learning objective

**Assignment-Specific Permissions:**

| Assignment | Permitted | Prohibited | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Memo | Brainstorming, grammar check | AI drafting, rewriting, summarizing | Conciseness is the skill |
| Report | Source finding (with verification), citation formatting | AI writing, AI summarizing sources | Evidence synthesis is the skill |
| Proposal | Generating counterarguments, proofreading | AI drafting the proposal or argument | Persuasion construction is the skill |
| Presentation Script | AI as practice audience for feedback | AI writing the script | Oral communication voice is the skill |

**Rubric:**
- Process Documentation (20%): drafts, revisions, AI Use Statement
- Audience & Purpose (25%): tone, format, audience-appropriateness
- Argument & Evidence (25%): logic, evidence quality, originality
- Writing Mechanics (15%): clarity, grammar, style
- Reflection (15%): self-awareness, AI use reflection

**Student Guide:** Includes permitted/prohibited/gray-area examples for each assignment type.

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** Option (C) is strong — it correctly differentiates AI permissions by learning objective, requires process documentation, dismisses unreliable detection tools, and raises the equity concern. However, option (D) was more complete: it added verifiable process evidence (not just self-reported statements, which are unenforceable), specified that detection tools are biased against non-native English speakers (not just unreliable in general), included student onboarding (learning and practicing the policy), and addressed policy evolution. These additions make the difference between a well-intentioned policy and an implementable one.

**Better Alternative:** Option **(D)** — includes everything in (C) plus verification, bias specificity, onboarding, and evolution planning.

**Grade: A** — Strong conceptual framework, but missed implementation and verification dimensions.

**Revised Rubric:**

**In-Class Verification:**

| Assignment | In-Class Component |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Memo | 20-min timed first draft (establishes baseline quality) |
| Report | Peer review session: explain evidence synthesis orally to 2 peers |
| Proposal | 10-min oral pitch + Q&A (verifies student can defend argument) |
| Presentation Script | Live delivery (performance itself) |

**Clarified Presentation Script Permissions:**

**Week 1 Policy Practice Workshop (45 min):**

- 15 min: Class discussion, clarify edge cases

### Summary Feedback

The student demonstrated strong and consistent performance. Framing was good — differentiating AI permissions by learning objective, requiring process documentation, and raising equity concerns were all sophisticated moves. The gap was not anticipating verification and onboarding challenges, which option (D) addressed. In judging, the student improved across cycles: from comprehensive issue identification in Cycle 1 (five issues) to equity-aware analysis in Cycle 2 (catching accessibility, update mechanism, and communication gaps). The draft fabrication concern (option A in Cycle 3) is a real issue the student chose not to pursue — this could have been addressed with timestamped draft submissions in a version-controlled tool. Steering was consistently excellent: corrections were specific, multi-dimensional (addressing rubric weighting, verification design, workshop content, accommodation details in structured requests), and produced a genuinely implementable policy framework. The classified scenarios workshop and the accommodation plan were particularly strong additions. To improve, the student should bring the same equity awareness shown in Cycle 2 to the initial framing phase, and consider adversarial scenarios (how could a determined student game this system?) earlier in the design process.
