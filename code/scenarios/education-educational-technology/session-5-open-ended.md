## PHASE 1 — FRAMING

### Raw Problem Presented to Student

> Develop a comprehensive AI-use policy and accompanying pedagogical framework for a 200-level undergraduate "Business Communication" course. The instructor is "not anti-AI" but worries that students are "submitting ChatGPT output as their own work" and "not learning to write." The course requires four major writing assignments (memo, report, proposal, presentation script). The university has a generic AI policy that says "use is permitted with disclosure" but the instructor finds this "too vague to be useful." Some students say AI tools help them "overcome writer's block" while others admit to "just prompting and submitting." Deliver a course-specific AI policy document, a revised assignment design for each of the four assignments that integrates AI use productively, a student-facing guide explaining permitted and prohibited uses with examples, and an assessment rubric that can distinguish AI-assisted work from AI-generated work.

### Student's Framing Response

> **Assumption: The course learning outcomes include: (1) write clear, audience-appropriate business documents, (2) construct persuasive arguments with evidence, (3) adapt communication style to context, and (4) give and receive constructive feedback on writing. AI use should be evaluated against these specific outcomes.**
>
> **Constraint: The policy must work for students with varying levels of AI access — some have paid ChatGPT Plus subscriptions, others use free tiers, and some may not use AI at all. The policy cannot create an advantage for those who pay more.**
>
> **Constraint: AI-detection tools (GPTZero, Turnitin AI detection) have documented false-positive rates and bias against non-native English speakers. The policy should not rely on detection as an enforcement mechanism.**
>
> **Question: The four assignments serve different learning goals — the memo practices conciseness, the report practices evidence synthesis, the proposal practices persuasion, the presentation script practices oral communication structure. AI's role should be different for each based on what skill each assignment develops.**

### AI Solution (Generated from Student's Framing)

**Course AI Policy Document:**

*Philosophy:* AI tools are treated as professional writing aids, similar to grammar checkers or style guides. The goal is to develop students' writing and thinking skills, with AI as a support — not a replacement — for the student's own intellectual work.

*Core Principles:*
1. **Transparency:** Students must disclose any AI tool use, including the nature and extent of use, in a brief "AI Use Statement" appended to each assignment.
2. **Process Over Product:** Grades reflect the student's thinking and revision process, not only the quality of the final document.
3. **Differentiated Use:** AI permissions vary by assignment based on the skill being developed.

**Assignment-Specific AI Permissions:**

| Assignment | AI Permitted Uses | AI Prohibited Uses | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Memo (conciseness) | Brainstorming topic ideas, grammar check after drafting | AI drafting, AI rewriting, AI summarization of source material | Students must practice distilling their own thinking into concise language |
| Report (evidence synthesis) | Locating sources (with verification), formatting citations | AI writing any section, AI summarizing sources (must read and summarize in own words) | Evidence evaluation and synthesis is the core skill |
| Proposal (persuasion) | Generating counterarguments to stress-test the proposal, proofreading | AI drafting the proposal, AI generating the persuasive argument | Constructing persuasive arguments is the learning target |
| Presentation Script (oral structure) | AI used as a practice audience (submit script to AI for feedback before class) | AI writing the script | Students must develop their oral communication voice |

**Student-Facing Guide (excerpt):**
*Permitted Example:* "I used ChatGPT to brainstorm 5 possible memo topics, then selected one and wrote the memo entirely myself."
*Prohibited Example:* "I pasted the assignment prompt into ChatGPT, edited the output, and submitted it."
*Gray Area Example:* "I wrote a draft, then asked ChatGPT to suggest improvements. I accepted some suggestions and rejected others." → This is permitted for the proposal (stress-testing) but prohibited for the memo (rewriting).

**Assessment Rubric:**
- Process Documentation (20%): quality of drafts, revisions, and AI Use Statement
- Audience & Purpose (25%): appropriate tone, format, and framing for the stated audience
- Argument & Evidence (25%): logical structure, evidence quality, originality of analysis
- Writing Mechanics (15%): clarity, grammar, professional style
- Reflection & Metacognition (15%): AI Use Statement quality, self-awareness of writing process

### Framing Feedback

**Criticism:** This is an unusually strong framing response. The student explicitly connected AI policy to learning outcomes, raised the equity concern about differential AI access, correctly dismissed AI-detection tools as unreliable, and recognized that AI's role should differ by assignment based on the skill being developed. The only gap is that the student did not address how the policy will be communicated and practiced — a policy document alone does not change behavior. Students need guided practice with the policy boundaries (e.g., an in-class exercise where they practice using AI within the permitted boundaries and reflect on the experience). The student also did not question how the instructor will verify the AI Use Statement's accuracy.

**Better Alternative Framing:**
> "All of the above, plus: Question: How will students practice applying the policy before the first graded assignment? A policy document alone won't change behavior — students need guided practice. Constraint: The AI Use Statement creates a verification problem — how does the instructor know the disclosure is accurate? Consider pairing the statement with process evidence (draft history, revision logs) rather than relying on self-report alone."

**Grade: A** — Comprehensive framing with learning outcomes alignment, equity awareness, and detection tool critique. Missed policy communication and verification.

**In-Class Verification Components:**

| Assignment | In-Class Component | Purpose |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|

The verification gap was the most important catch: a self-reported AI Use Statement without corroborating evidence is unenforceable. The presentation script permission ambiguity was a nuanced observation about the blurry line between AI feedback and AI writing. However, the student did not address equity in the in-class components: timed handwriting for the memo disadvantages students with writing disabilities or those who draft digitally. The student also did not consider that draft history can be fabricated (a student could write the final version first and reverse-engineer draft versions).

**Better Alternative Judgement:**
> "Gap: Process Documentation is only 20% despite being the core anti-misuse mechanism. Gap: AI Use Statements are unverifiable without corroborating process evidence or in-class components. Error: Presentation script AI permissions are ambiguous — feedback vs rewriting line is unclear. Gap: Draft history can be fabricated. Gap: Timed in-class writing may disadvantage students with disabilities."

**Grade: A** — Sophisticated analysis catching three important design flaws, missed equity and fabrication concerns.

#### Steering Feedback

**Criticism:** The steering was precise and well-structured. Increasing process weight with specific evidence requirements (annotated drafts, revision rationale, interaction logs) was a strong move. The in-class verification table was creative and practical — each component is matched to the assignment's learning objective and provides a natural cross-check. The presentation script clarification with concrete examples was exactly what the policy needed. The student could have also addressed accessibility of in-class components and draft fabrication.

**Grade: A** — Specific, creative, and pedagogically sophisticated steering.

#### Updated AI Output

**Week 1 Policy Practice Workshop (45 minutes):**

*Agenda:*
1. (10 min) Instructor presents the AI policy with the rationale and examples
2. (20 min) Small group exercise: classify 5 scenarios

*Sample Scenarios:*
- Scenario A: "I asked ChatGPT to generate an outline for my report, then wrote each section myself." → Permitted for Report (brainstorming), as long as the writing is original.
- Scenario B: "I wrote my memo, then pasted it into ChatGPT and asked it to 'make it more concise.' I used the shortened version." → Prohibited for Memo (AI rewrote the text, which is the core skill being assessed).
- Scenario C: "English isn't my first language. I used Grammarly's AI suggestions to fix grammar throughout my proposal." → Permitted for all assignments (grammar correction is like a spell-checker, not content generation).
- Scenario D: "I recorded myself explaining my proposal idea to ChatGPT and asked it to 'turn this into a professional proposal.' I edited the result." → Prohibited (AI generated the document structure and language).
- Scenario E: "I used ChatGPT to find three counterarguments to my proposal, then wrote rebuttals to each in my own words." → Permitted for Proposal (using AI to identify counterarguments to stress-test your argument).

3. (15 min) Class discussion: groups share disagreements, instructor clarifies edge cases, class collectively refines understanding

*Deliverable:* Students submit a brief reflection (200 words) describing one personal "gray area" scenario they anticipate encountering and how they plan to handle it.

**Mid-Semester Policy Review:**
- Anonymous survey (Week 7): "Describe an AI-related situation you encountered that the policy didn't clearly address."
- Instructor reviews survey responses, publishes updated FAQ
- Policy Questions channel on course discussion board (ongoing)
- Commitment: "This policy is a living document. We will update it together as the semester progresses."

#### Judging Feedback

**Criticism:** The student identified two important gaps: the onboarding problem and the update mechanism. Both reflect sophisticated thinking about policy implementation — a well-designed policy that students don't understand or that becomes outdated quickly is functionally equivalent to no policy. The workshop design with classified scenarios is an effective pedagogical tool. The student did not flag that the policy still has a potential equity issue with the in-class timed memo draft: students with documented disabilities, students whose primary language is not English, or students with test anxiety may perform disproportionately worse in timed conditions, making the "baseline quality" comparison unfair.

**Better Alternative Judgement:**
> "Gap: No student onboarding — the policy needs a practice activity before graded work begins. Gap: No update mechanism for a rapidly evolving technology landscape. Gap: Timed in-class memo draft may disadvantage students with disabilities or non-native English speakers, creating an equity issue in the verification system."

**Grade: A** — Strong implementation thinking, missed the equity dimension of timed writing.

#### Steering Feedback

**Criticism:** The workshop design was well-structured and pedagogically effective — the five scenarios progress from clear-cut to genuinely ambiguous, which builds classification skills. Asking students to anticipate their own gray areas was a clever engagement technique. The mid-semester review mechanism with an anonymous survey and published FAQ is practical and responsive. This was strong steering throughout.

**Grade: A** — Creative, practical, and pedagogically grounded.
