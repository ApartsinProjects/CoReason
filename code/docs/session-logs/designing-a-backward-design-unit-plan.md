## Challenge Overview

This challenge tests the student's ability to engage in co-reasoning with an AI system across three phases: framing (problem definition), judging (critical evaluation of AI output), and steering (directing AI improvement). The "Designing a Backward Design Unit Plan" challenge focuses on Education and Instructional Design and requires students to demonstrate iterative practice skills in human-AI collaboration.

### Backward Lesson Plan for Effective Communication Skills Training Program

#### Overall Goal
By the end of the three-week training program, employees will demonstrate improved communication skills in both verbal and written formats, enhancing their ability to convey ideas clearly, actively listen, and provide constructive feedback in a professional environment.

#### Desired Learning Outcomes
1. Employees will be able to articulate their ideas clearly and effectively in verbal presentations.
2. Employees will demonstrate active listening skills by summarizing and responding appropriately to others' contributions.
3. Employees will produce clear and concise written communications, such as emails and reports, appropriate for a professional setting.
4. Employees will engage in constructive feedback sessions, applying techniques learned during the training to give and receive feedback effectively.

#### Assessments
To evaluate learning, we will implement both formative and summative assessments throughout the program:

1. **Formative Assessments:**
   - **Peer Feedback Sessions:** During practice presentations, employees will provide and receive feedback from peers to refine their communication skills.
   - **Reflective Journals:** Participants will maintain a journal where they reflect on their learning experiences and self-assess their communication strengths and areas for improvement.
   - **Role-Playing Scenarios:** Employees will engage in role-playing exercises where they must apply active listening and feedback techniques, followed by group discussions to assess understanding.

2. **Summative Assessments:**
   - **Final Presentation:** At the end of the program, each employee will deliver a presentation on a topic of their choice, evaluated based on clarity, organization, and engagement.
   - **Written Communication Task:** Employees will submit a written report that will be assessed for clarity, conciseness, and professionalism.
   - **Feedback Review:** A final assessment of how well employees apply feedback techniques during a group discussion, measured against a rubric.

#### Instructional Activities
The following instructional activities will be designed to meet the desired outcomes over the three-week program:

**Week 1: Foundations of Communication**
- **Workshop on Verbal Communication:** Introduction to key principles of effective verbal communication, including clarity, tone, and body language.
- **Active Listening Exercise:** Participants will engage in paired activities, practicing active listening techniques and summarizing peers' points to reinforce skills.

**Week 2: Written Communication Skills**
- **Writing Workshops:** Focused sessions on professional writing, covering structure, tone, and clarity in emails and reports.
- **Peer Review Sessions:** Participants will draft a professional email or report and exchange with peers for constructive feedback, emphasizing the importance of clarity and conciseness.

**Week 3: Integration and Application**
- **Presentation Skills Training:** Instruction on how to design and deliver engaging presentations, incorporating visual aids and handling Q&A sessions.
- **Role-Playing Feedback Scenarios:** Participants will practice giving and receiving feedback in small groups, followed by discussions to consolidate learning.
- **Final Project Preparation:** Employees will prepare for their final presentation, utilizing feedback from earlier activities to enhance their skills.

### Conclusion
This backward lesson plan outlines a comprehensive approach to developing effective communication skills in employees. By clearly defining the overall goal, learning outcomes, assessments, and instructional activities, this program aims to foster significant improvements in both verbal and written communication within a professional context.

## Pedagogical Summary

This session log for "Designing a Backward Design Unit Plan" demonstrates the co-reasoning assessment model in action. The three-phase structure — framing, judging, and steering — maps to critical competencies for effective human-AI collaboration:

1. **Framing** (problem definition): Tests whether students can identify ambiguities and structure problems before delegating to AI. This prevents the common pitfall of accepting AI output generated from poorly specified prompts.

2. **Judging** (critical evaluation): Tests whether students can identify errors, gaps, and limitations in AI-generated content. This develops the "trust but verify" mindset essential for responsible AI use.

3. **Steering** (directed improvement): Tests whether students can provide actionable feedback to improve AI output. This develops the metacognitive skill of knowing what good output looks like and how to guide AI toward it.

The practice mode provides immediate feedback after each phase, enabling iterative learning. The open-ended/open-ended response format requires fully independent articulation of analytical reasoning.

This challenge, drawn from Education and Instructional Design, demonstrates that co-reasoning skills are transferable across domains — the same framing-judging-steering cycle applies whether the student is working with AI on algorithms, physics, ethics, or pedagogy.
